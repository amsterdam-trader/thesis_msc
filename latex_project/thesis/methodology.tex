\section{Methodology}\label{sec:methodology}

We consider the empirical strategy used to answer the seasonal
extremal-dependence question of
Section~\ref{sec:intro-rq}. The strategy is built around four
ingredients: (i) construction of extreme observations from the
hourly \(\mathrm{FX}\) series; (ii) marginal standardisation across
stations; (iii) estimation of pairwise extremal dependence; and
(iv) summarisation as a function of distance, separately for winter
and summer. Each ingredient corresponds to one subsection below.
Operational numerical choices (e.g.\ block size in hours, threshold
quantile, bootstrap block length) are recorded in
Section~\ref{sec:empirical-design}, so that the methodology section
remains valid when those numbers are tuned.

\subsection{Overview of the empirical
strategy}\label{sec:meth-overview}

The goal is to estimate the seasonal dependence--distance curves
\[
    \chi^{(c)}(d) \;=\; \mathbb{E}\!\bigl[\,\chi_{ij}^{(c)} \,\big|\,
    d_{ij} = d\,\bigr],
    \qquad c \in \{\mathrm{W},\mathrm{S}\},
\]
introduced in Section~\ref{sec:evt-dependence-distance}, together
with the analogous curve in \(\theta_{ij}^{(c)}\). The seasonal
comparison is then the pair of estimates
\(\widehat\chi^{(\mathrm{W})}(d)\) and
\(\widehat\chi^{(\mathrm{S})}(d)\), displayed on the same axes with
bootstrap uncertainty bands and supplemented by a pointwise test of
the null hypothesis \(\chi^{(\mathrm{W})}(d) = \chi^{(\mathrm{S})}(d)\).

The main estimation route is via \emph{block maxima}: one block-
maximum observation per station per block, with the block defined
inside a single season. The threshold-exceedance (POT) route is
treated as a robustness check
(Section~\ref{sec:meth-extremes-pot}). This ordering is motivated
by three considerations. Block maxima have a clean GEV-limit
interpretation that does not require declustering of hourly
observations; the bivariate extremal coefficient is naturally
estimated from block maxima via the F-madogram; and a single
block per season per station per year is the smallest unit that
keeps the methodology tractable for an MSc-scale project.

\subsection{Station and sample selection}\label{sec:meth-filter}

We restrict the panel \(\{X_{i,t}\}\) by the following filters,
applied in order.

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Keep only Dutch land and coastal KNMI stations with
          \(\mathrm{FX}\) reduced to the 10\,m reference height
          (Section~\ref{sec:emp-stations}).
    \item Discard hours where \(\mathrm{FX}\) is missing or flagged
          invalid by KNMI quality control.
    \item Discard station-season-years that fail the within-season
          completeness threshold of
          Section~\ref{sec:emp-missing}.
    \item Define the balanced station panel as the set of stations
          satisfying steps 1--3 in every winter and every summer of
          the chosen sample window. Stations that fail this
          requirement are retained only in the larger unbalanced
          panel used for robustness
          (Section~\ref{sec:rob-stations}).
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Seasonal stratification}\label{sec:meth-seasons}

We split the hourly panel into two non-overlapping seasonal
subsamples,
\[
    \mathcal{T}_{\mathrm{W}}
    \;=\; \{t : \mathrm{month}(t) \in \{12,1,2\}\},
    \qquad
    \mathcal{T}_{\mathrm{S}}
    \;=\; \{t : \mathrm{month}(t) \in \{6,7,8\}\}.
\]
December of calendar year \(y\) is attached to winter year
\(y+1\), so that each winter is contiguous in calendar time. The
remaining months (MAM and SON) are dropped from the main analysis.

\subsection{Construction of extreme observations (main route:
block maxima)}\label{sec:meth-extremes}

For each station \(i\), each season \(c\) and each year \(y\), we
construct one or more block maxima of \(X_{i,t}\) over consecutive
hours within \(\mathcal{T}_c\). Let \(n\) denote the block length
in hours and let \(\mathcal{B}_{i,n,k}^{(c)}\) denote the
\(k\)-th non-overlapping block of \(n\) hours inside season \(c\)
of year \(y\). The block maximum is
\[
    M_{i,b}^{(c)}
    \;=\;
    \max\!\bigl\{ X_{i,t}\,:\, t \in \mathcal{B}_{i,n,k}^{(c)} \bigr\},
\]
where the index \(b\) ranges over the available
(year, season-block) combinations for station \(i\). A block is
discarded if its within-block missing fraction exceeds the
threshold in Section~\ref{sec:emp-missing}.

Two natural block sizes are considered:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \emph{Seasonal block} (\(n = |\mathcal{T}_c|\)): one
          observation per station per season per year. This is
          the cleanest block-maximum from the GEV perspective.
    \item \emph{Monthly-within-season block} (\(n \approx 24\times
          30\) hours): roughly three observations per station per
          season per year. This gives a larger effective sample
          at the cost of a less clean block-maximum interpretation.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Construction of extreme observations (alternative
route: threshold exceedances)}\label{sec:meth-extremes-pot}

As an alternative we construct, for each station \(i\) and each
season \(c\), peaks over a high station- and season-specific
threshold \(u_i^{(c)}\), set as a fixed empirical quantile of
\(\{X_{i,t}: t \in \mathcal{T}_c\}\). Exceedances are
\[
    Y_{i,t}^{(c)} \;=\; X_{i,t} - u_i^{(c)}
    \quad\text{for}\quad
    X_{i,t} > u_i^{(c)},\ t \in \mathcal{T}_c.
\]
The exceedance sequence at each station is declustered via the
automatic scheme of \citet{TODO-ferro2003}, which is based on the
extremal index estimated from inter-exceedance times. The result
is an approximately independent set of station-level cluster
peaks per (station, season). This route is reported as a robustness
check; it is not the primary route because the dependence
estimators below treat the within-season hourly time index as the
natural pairing axis, which is cleaner for block maxima than for
declustered exceedances.

\subsection{Marginal standardisation}\label{sec:meth-marginals}

To remove differences in marginal scale between stations and
between seasons, we transform each station's series to a common
\((0,1)\) margin. Two transforms are considered.

\paragraph{Empirical rank transform (main).}
For each (station, season), set
\[
    \widehat U_{i,t}^{(c)}
    \;=\; \widehat F_i^{(c)}(X_{i,t}),
    \qquad t \in \mathcal{T}_c,
\]
where \(\widehat F_i^{(c)}\) is the within-season empirical CDF at
station \(i\). This is the default because it makes no
parametric assumption about the marginal distribution.

\paragraph{Parametric tail transform (robustness).}
For each (station, season), fit either a GEV to the block-maxima
series \(\{M_{i,b}^{(c)}\}_b\) or a GPD to the exceedances
\(\{Y_{i,t}^{(c)}\}\), depending on which extreme-construction
route is being used, and transform to a unit-Fréchet margin via
\(Z_{i,t}^{(c)} = -1/\log\widehat G_i^{(c)}(X_{i,t})\). This
exploits the extreme-value limit but is sensitive to the marginal
fit; we report it as a robustness check (Section~\ref{sec:rob-marginals}).

\subsection{Pairwise extremal dependence
estimation}\label{sec:meth-dependence}

We estimate the season-stratified pairwise dependence summaries
\(\chi_{ij}^{(c)}\), \(\theta_{ij}^{(c)}\) and \(\bar\chi_{ij}^{(c)}\)
defined in Section~\ref{sec:evt-pairwise}. The estimators are
empirical and apply directly to the rank-transformed series
\(\widehat U_{i,t}^{(c)}\).

\paragraph{Empirical \(\chi\) at level \(u\).}
For every pair \((i,j)\) and every \(u\) close to 1 we compute
\[
    \widehat\chi_{ij}^{(c)}(u)
    \;=\;
    \frac{\#\bigl\{t \in \mathcal{T}_c :
       \widehat U_{i,t}^{(c)} > u,\,
       \widehat U_{j,t}^{(c)} > u\bigr\}}
         {\#\bigl\{t \in \mathcal{T}_c :
       \widehat U_{i,t}^{(c)} > u\bigr\}}.
\]
We report \(\widehat\chi_{ij}^{(c)}(u)\) on a grid of \(u\)
following \citet{TODO-wadsworth2012}, together with a chosen
reference level set in Section~\ref{sec:empirical-design}.

\paragraph{Empirical extremal coefficient via the F-madogram.}
On the block-maxima series \(\{M_{i,b}^{(c)}\}_b\) we use the
F-madogram estimator of \citet{TODO-davison2012},
\[
    \widehat\nu_F\bigl(\,i,j;\,c\,\bigr)
    \;=\;
    \frac{1}{2B^{(c)}} \sum_{b=1}^{B^{(c)}}
    \bigl| \widehat F_i^{(c)}(M_{i,b}^{(c)}) -
           \widehat F_j^{(c)}(M_{j,b}^{(c)}) \bigr|,
\]
and convert to the extremal coefficient via
\(\widehat\theta_{ij}^{(c)} = (1 + 2\widehat\nu_F)/(1 - 2\widehat\nu_F)\).
Here \(B^{(c)}\) is the number of available season-\(c\) blocks
for the pair on a pairwise-complete basis.

\paragraph{Chibar diagnostic.}
For each pair we also compute the finite-level
\(\widehat{\bar\chi}_{ij}^{(c)}(u)\) on the same grid as
\(\widehat\chi_{ij}^{(c)}(u)\), reported jointly for diagnosing
asymptotic independence.

\subsection{Distance summary}\label{sec:meth-distance}

The pair-level estimates above are noisy. We summarise them along
geographical distance \(d_{ij} = \mathrm{dist}(s_i,s_j)\) by
fitting, for each season \(c\), a one-dimensional smoother
\[
    \widehat\chi^{(c)}(d)
    \;=\; \mathrm{smoother}\!\Bigl(\bigl\{(\widehat\chi_{ij}^{(c)},\,
        d_{ij})\bigr\}_{i<j}\Bigr).
\]
We use a local-linear kernel smoother with Gaussian kernel; the
bandwidth is selected by leave-one-out cross-validation within
each season and is reported in
Section~\ref{sec:emp-extremes}. The smoother is not the object of
interest in its own right — it is a device to make the seasonal
comparison legible. The raw pair-level scatter is shown alongside
the smoothed curve so that the reader can judge whether the
smoother is hiding structure.

\subsection{Winter--summer comparison}\label{sec:meth-comparison}

The headline empirical comparison combines three reports.

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Overlay.} The two smoothed curves
          \(\widehat\chi^{(\mathrm{W})}(d)\) and
          \(\widehat\chi^{(\mathrm{S})}(d)\) on a single panel
          with their bootstrap bands.
    \item \textbf{Scalar summaries.} The values
          \(\widehat\chi^{(c)}(d_0)\) at a small set of reference
          distances \(d_0\) (e.g.\ \(50\), \(100\), \(200\) km),
          with bootstrap percentile intervals.
    \item \textbf{Difference curve.} The pointwise difference
          \(\widehat\chi^{(\mathrm{W})}(d) - \widehat\chi^{(\mathrm{S})}(d)\)
          with a bootstrap band and a permutation test of the null
          hypothesis \(\chi^{(\mathrm{W})}(d) = \chi^{(\mathrm{S})}(d)\).
\end{enumerate}

The permutation test is constructed by randomly relabelling the
seasonal indicator across whole seasons (not across individual
hours), which preserves the within-season dependence structure that
the bootstrap also needs to preserve.

\subsection{Uncertainty quantification}\label{sec:meth-uncertainty}

Hourly wind gusts within a season exhibit strong serial dependence
(storm clusters); the block-maxima series also display modest
serial dependence between consecutive years. We therefore use a
\emph{block bootstrap} that resamples consecutive blocks of length
\(L\) hours from the panel \(\{X_{i,t}: t \in \mathcal{T}_c\}\),
re-runs the rank transform and the pairwise estimators inside each
bootstrap replication, and produces a percentile interval over
\(B\) replications. The block length \(L\) must exceed a typical
storm duration; \citet{TODO-fawcett2006} and
\citet{TODO-ferro2003} provide informal guidance for the
appropriate scale. The numerical values of \(L\) and \(B\) are
recorded in Section~\ref{sec:emp-inference}.

\subsection{Robustness checks}\label{sec:meth-robustness}

The robustness panel is detailed in
Section~\ref{sec:robustness}. The main exercises are:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Balanced vs.\ unbalanced station panel.
    \item Block size: monthly-within-season vs.\ seasonal block.
    \item Threshold-exceedance route as an alternative to block
          maxima.
    \item Marginal standardisation: rank transform vs.\ parametric
          GEV/GPD tail transform.
    \item Calendar-time sample split (early half vs.\ late half),
          as the secondary long-run analysis.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Optional: parametric max-stable
fitting}\label{sec:meth-maxstable}

If time permits we fit a parametric max-stable process — for
example the Brown--Resnick, Schlather or extremal Gaussian family
\citep{TODO-davison2012,TODO-dehaan2006} — to the block-maxima
series by pairwise composite likelihood
\citep{TODO-padoan2010}, one season at a time. The fitted
dependence function is then compared with the nonparametric
\(\widehat\chi^{(c)}(d)\) of Section~\ref{sec:meth-distance}. This
step is optional and is not required to answer the main research
question. A fully covariate-dependent (season-indexed) max-stable
model in the spirit of \citet{TODO-huser-genton2016} is well
beyond the scope of an MSc thesis and is recorded only as a
future-work direction in Section~\ref{sec:conclusion-future}.

\subsection{Limitations}\label{sec:meth-limitations}

Three limitations are worth flagging at this point.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \emph{Within-season stationarity.} The empirical
          strategy treats the field as approximately stationary
          within season \(c\). This is the methodological reason
          for the seasonal stratification, but it is not exact:
          there is residual non-stationarity inside a single DJF
          season (e.g.\ a December warming trend), which the
          robustness sample-split addresses only partially.
    \item \emph{Asymptotic dependence assumption.} The estimators
          \(\widehat\chi_{ij}^{(c)}\) and
          \(\widehat\theta_{ij}^{(c)}\) treat finite-\(u\)
          quantities as informative about the asymptotic
          quantities. \citet{TODO-wadsworth2012} show that finite-
          level dependence can decay to zero at the very top of the
          tail; we partially address this by reporting on a grid of
          \(u\) and by including the \(\bar\chi\) diagnostic.
    \item \emph{Isotropy.} The distance smoother treats the
          dependence as a function of the scalar distance only.
          Anisotropy due to prevailing wind direction is a
          plausible second-order effect; it is examined in
          Section~\ref{sec:rob-stations} as a robustness check.
\end{itemize}
