\section{Methodology}\label{sec:methodology}

The previous chapters motivate a comparison of the spatial extent of wind-gust extremes between winter and summer. This chapter defines the parametric objects used for that comparison. The three fitted models differ in their stochastic assumptions, but each is translated to the same distance-indexed upper tail-dependence curve \(\chi(d)\), and the seasonal question is answered on that scale. The exposition follows one order throughout --- model specification, then estimation, then bootstrap, then simulation evaluation --- and the three methods are treated symmetrically, so that any difference in their conclusions reflects their assumptions rather than the procedure around them.

\subsection{Overview}\label{sec:meth-overview}

The pipeline, with the section fixing each step, is: marginal standardisation \(X_{itc}\mapsto(\widehat U_{itc},\widehat Z_{itc})\) per station--season (Sect.~\ref{sec:meth-marginals}), checked in Sect.~\ref{sec:meth-marg-diag}; temporal-clustering diagnostics (Sect.~\ref{sec:meth-temporal}); the curve \(\chi_{M,c}(d)\), contrast \(\Delta_M^{\chi}(d)\), and grid \(\mathcal D\) (Sect.~\ref{sec:meth-chi}); model-free pairwise diagnostics, retained as checks only (Sect.~\ref{sec:meth-diagnostics}); the three models with closed-form \(\chi_{M,c}(d)\) (Sect.~\ref{sec:meth-models}); pairwise composite and copula estimation by season (Sect.~\ref{sec:meth-cl}); the seasonal comparison and the agreement rule (Sect.~\ref{sec:meth-comparison}); the season-year block bootstrap (Sect.~\ref{sec:meth-inference}); and the Monte Carlo benchmark (Sect.~\ref{sec:meth-simulation}, design in Sect.~\ref{sec:simulation}). Margins precede dependence, time precedes space, and the curve is fixed before the models, so that the models are three answers to one question.

\subsection{Notation and sampling frame}\label{sec:meth-notation}

Indices run over methods, seasons, stations, and station pairs,
\begin{equation}
  M\in\mathcal M=\{\mathrm{BR},\mathrm{ExtT},\mathrm{tCop}\},
  \qquad c\in\{\mathrm W,\mathrm S\},
  \qquad i=1,\dots,N,\quad N=33,
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal P=\{(i,j):1\le i<j\le N\},\quad |\mathcal P|=\tbinom{N}{2}=528,
  \qquad
  d_{ij}=\lVert s_i-s_j\rVert,\quad
  \mathcal T^{ij}_c=\mathcal T_{i,c}\cap\mathcal T_{j,c},
\end{equation}
where \(s_i\in\mathbb R^2\) are the station locations, \(\mathcal T_{i,c}\) the days station \(i\) is observed in season \(c\), \(n^{(c)}_{ij}=|\mathcal T^{ij}_c|\), and \(d_{\max}=\max_{i<j}d_{ij}\approx327\)~km (Sect.~\ref{sec:data}). Each season is the union of \(n_y\) season-year blocks \(\mathcal B_{c,y}\), with winters contiguous over December--February; these blocks are the resampling units of Sect.~\ref{sec:meth-inference}. The three-index symbol \(X_{itc}\) denotes the daily maximum gust and coincides with the \(X^{(c)}_{i,t}\) of Sect.~\ref{sec:data}. Every estimator below is computed once for each season.

\subsection{Marginal standardisation}\label{sec:meth-marginals}

For each station and season a GEV distribution \(\widehat F_{ic}\) is fitted to \(\{X_{itc}:t\in\mathcal T_{i,c}\}\) by maximum likelihood, giving \(2N=66\) fits, and the data are mapped to a common scale,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:meth-standardisation}
  \widehat U_{itc}=\widehat F_{ic}(X_{itc}),
  \qquad
  \widehat Z_{itc}=-\frac{1}{\log \widehat U_{itc}},
\end{equation}
so that \(\widehat U_{itc}\) is approximately standard uniform and \(\widehat Z_{itc}\) approximately unit Fr\'echet. A rank scale \(\widetilde U_{itc}=\{n^{(c)}_i+1\}^{-1}\sum_{t'}\mathbf 1\{X_{it'c}\le X_{itc}\}\) is kept in parallel as a model-free standardisation. The two max-stable models are written on the unit-Fr\'echet scale and the copula on the uniform scale,
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{BR},\,\mathrm{ExtT}\ \text{use}\ \widehat Z_{itc},
  \qquad
  \mathrm{tCop}\ \text{uses}\ \widehat U_{itc},
\end{equation}
with \(\widehat Z_{itc}\) a fixed monotone transform of \(\widehat U_{itc}\) and the diagnostics of Sects.~\ref{sec:meth-temporal} and~\ref{sec:meth-diagnostics} using \(\widetilde U_{itc}\).

By Sklar's theorem the transform \eqref{eq:meth-standardisation} removes the station--season margin and leaves the copula, so a residual winter--summer difference is a difference in dependence rather than in local wind climate, and because all three methods use the same standardised data, none gains an advantage from preprocessing. The GEV here is a standardisation device for the upper tail, not a claim that daily maxima are exact block maxima; its adequacy is a matter of fit. A poor marginal fit at station \(i\) propagates identically into every method through \(\widehat U_{itc}\) and \(\widehat Z_{itc}\), and marginal estimation uncertainty is carried into the final intervals by re-estimating the margins inside the bootstrap (Sect.~\ref{sec:meth-inference}).

\subsection{Marginal diagnostics}\label{sec:meth-marg-diag}

Each of the \(66\) fits is judged graphically, since serial dependence within storms (Sect.~\ref{sec:meth-temporal}) invalidates independence-based goodness-of-fit \(p\)-values. The checks are GEV quantile--quantile plots, with the upper tail decisive; the uniformity of \(\widehat U_{itc}\); and \(\widehat U_{itc}\) against \(\widetilde U_{itc}\) above the \(0.9\) level, which localises the upper-tail misfit that matters for the dependence analysis. The results chapter reports a representative selection and the worst cases by the screening statistic \(\max_x|\widehat F_{ic}(x)-\widetilde F_{ic}(x)|\). Because a poor fit at station \(i\) tilts all \(N-1\) pairwise contributions containing \(i\) in every method, the dependence results are read conditional on these checks, with the rank scale as the immune fallback.

\subsection{Temporal clustering}\label{sec:meth-temporal}

All estimators treat days as replicates of the spatial field, but storms persist over consecutive days, so the replicates cluster. For station \((i,c)\) and level \(u\), with consecutiveness defined within a season-year block,
\begin{equation}
  \widehat p^{(c)}_{u,i}
  =\frac{\#\{t:\ \widetilde U_{itc}>u,\ \widetilde U_{i,t+1,c}>u\}}
  {\#\{t:\ \widetilde U_{itc}>u\}},
  \qquad u\in\{0.90,0.95,0.975\},
\end{equation}
with independence benchmark \(1-u\); the all-lag summary is the extremal index \(\theta_{\mathrm T}\in(0,1]\) of \citet{ferro2003} (temporal, not to be confused with the spatial \(\theta_{M,c}(d)\)). The data are not declustered, since deleting days would remove whole \(N\)-station fields and the pairwise objectives remain valid for dependent replicates under stationarity and mixing \citep{huserdavison2014}; the consequence of clustering for uncertainty is instead handled by resampling whole season-years (Sect.~\ref{sec:meth-inference}). The diagnostic quantifies serial dependence across the level grid but does not remove it.

\subsection{The common comparison object \texorpdfstring{$\chi(d)$}{chi(d)}}\label{sec:meth-chi}

For a fitted model \(M\), season \(c\), and uniform-scale pair \((U_i,U_j)\) at distance \(d\),
\begin{align}
  \chi_{M,c}(d)
   & =\lim_{u\to1}\Pr_M\!\bigl(U_i>u\mid U_j>u;\ d_{ij}=d\bigr),
  \label{eq:meth-chi-def}                                        \\[2pt]
  \Delta_M^{\chi}(d)
   & =\chi_{M,\mathrm W}(d)-\chi_{M,\mathrm S}(d).
  \label{eq:meth-delta-def}
\end{align}
For the two max-stable models the bivariate copula is an extreme-value copula with \(C(u,u)=u^{\theta_{M,c}(d)}\), so the curve follows from the extremal coefficient,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:meth-chi-theta}
  \chi_{M,c}(d)=2-\theta_{M,c}(d),\qquad M\in\{\mathrm{BR},\mathrm{ExtT}\}
\end{equation}
(Appendices~\ref{app:exponent}--\ref{app:tail-dependence}); for the \(t\) copula the limit \eqref{eq:meth-chi-def} is evaluated directly (Sect.~\ref{sec:meth-tcop}). Curves are read on a fixed grid and summarised at selected distances,
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal D=\{d_1,\dots,d_G\}\subset[0,d_{\max}],
  \qquad
  \mathcal D_0=\{50,100,200,300\}\ \text{km}\subset\mathcal D .
\end{equation}

% and confirm/justify D_0 from the empirical distribution of d_ij; keep
% D and D_0 identical in methodology, simulation, and code.

The quantity \(\chi_{M,c}(d)\) is the model's probability of a co-occurring extreme at distance \(d\), and \(\Delta_M^{\chi}(d)>0\) means model \(M\) implies a stronger, wider winter footprint at \(d\). It is the scale on which the models are compared because it is a functional of the copula alone \citep{sibuya1960,coles1999} and is therefore defined for every \(M\), whereas the internal parameters are not comparable across models --- the symbol \(\rho\) does not even denote the same object in all three (Sect.~\ref{sec:meth-br}). The curve is a limit while the data are finite-level, and all three models are asymptotically dependent, so none can represent dependence that vanishes only in the limit \citep{ledford1996}; conclusions are accordingly stated as comparisons of fitted curves over the observed distances and checked against the finite-level diagnostics below.

\subsection{Descriptive pairwise diagnostics}\label{sec:meth-diagnostics}

The following pairwise summaries are diagnostics and visual checks, not estimators of \(\chi(d)\) and not among the three methods: they are finite-level, level-dependent, and cross-correlated over the \(528\) pairs, so no formal inference attaches. From the rank scale, for \((i,j)\in\mathcal P\),
\begin{equation}
  \widehat\chi^{(c)}_{ij}(u)
  =\frac{2\,\#\{t\in\mathcal T^{ij}_c:\widetilde U_{itc}>u,\widetilde U_{jtc}>u\}}
  {\#\{t:\widetilde U_{itc}>u\}+\#\{t:\widetilde U_{jtc}>u\}},
  \qquad
  \widehat\theta^{(c)}_{ij}(u)=\frac{\log\widehat C^{(c)}_{ij}(u,u)}{\log u},
\end{equation}
the finite-level version of \eqref{eq:meth-chi-def} and the copula-diagonal extremal coefficient \citep{coles1999,schlather2003}; the \(F\)-madogram \citep{cooley2006} is recorded but not used. Plotted against distance per season and level, these give a model-free first look at whether the winter cloud sits above the summer one and, overlaid with the fitted finite-level curves, a check on the parametric fits. Two patterns are watched for: \(\widehat\chi^{(c)}_{ij}(u)\) falling towards \(1-u\) faster than any fitted curve allows, the signature of asymptotic independence that none of the models represents \citep{ledford1996,huserwadsworth2022}; and dependence on orientation or coast--inland membership rather than distance, which would question the isotropy all three models assume.

\subsection{The three parametric models}\label{sec:meth-models}

Each method has a season-specific parameter vector and is estimated by maximising a pairwise objective,
\begin{equation}
  \vartheta_{\mathrm{BR},c}=(\rho_{\mathrm{BR},c},\alpha_{\mathrm{BR},c}),\quad
  \vartheta_{\mathrm{ExtT},c}=(\rho_{\mathrm{ExtT},c},\alpha_{\mathrm{ExtT},c},\nu_{\mathrm{ExtT},c}),\quad
  \vartheta_{\mathrm{tCop},c}=(\rho_{\mathrm{tCop},c},\alpha_{\mathrm{tCop},c},\nu_{\mathrm{tCop},c}),
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}\label{eq:meth-argmax}
  \widehat\vartheta_{M,c}=\arg\max_{\vartheta_{M,c}\in\Theta_M}\ \ell_{M,c}(\vartheta_{M,c}),
  \qquad M\in\mathcal M,\ c\in\{\mathrm W,\mathrm S\}.
\end{equation}
Within each model's block the method subscript is suppressed, \(\rho_c:=\rho_{M,c}\) and so on. The set spans two axes: Brown--Resnick and extremal-\(t\) are both max-stable but impose different decay shapes, while the Student-\(t\) copula sits outside the max-stable class; all three assume isotropy and within-season stationarity. Each model below gives its dependence function, its curve \(\chi_{M,c}(d)\), its pairwise density, and its main limitation; estimation \eqref{eq:meth-argmax} is detailed in Sect.~\ref{sec:meth-cl}.

\subsubsection{Brown--Resnick}\label{sec:meth-br}\label{sec:meth-variogram}

With the powered-exponential variogram of the underlying Gaussian process \citep{brown1977,kabluchko2009} and \(a_c(d)=\sqrt{\gamma_c(d)}\),
\begin{equation}\label{eq:meth-br-chi}
  \gamma_c(d)=\Bigl(\frac{d}{\rho_c}\Bigr)^{\alpha_c},
  \qquad
  \theta_{\mathrm{BR},c}(d)=2\Phi\!\Bigl(\tfrac12\sqrt{\gamma_c(d)}\Bigr),
  \qquad
  \chi_{\mathrm{BR},c}(d)=2-\theta_{\mathrm{BR},c}(d),
\end{equation}
where \(\rho_c>0\), \(\alpha_c\in(0,2]\), \(\chi_{\mathrm{BR},c}(0)=1\), and \(\chi_{\mathrm{BR},c}(d)\to0\) as \(d\to\infty\) (Appendix~\ref{app:br-theta}). The bivariate law is of H\"usler--Reiss type \citep{husler1989}, \(\exp\{-V^{\mathrm{BR}}_d(z_i,z_j;\vartheta_{\mathrm{BR},c})\}\) with
\begin{equation}
  V^{\mathrm{BR}}_d(z_i,z_j)
  =\frac{1}{z_i}\Phi\!\Bigl(\tfrac{a_c(d)}{2}+\tfrac{1}{a_c(d)}\log\tfrac{z_j}{z_i}\Bigr)
  +\frac{1}{z_j}\Phi\!\Bigl(\tfrac{a_c(d)}{2}+\tfrac{1}{a_c(d)}\log\tfrac{z_i}{z_j}\Bigr),
\end{equation}
and the bivariate density \(f_{\mathrm{BR},ij}=(\partial_{z_i}V\,\partial_{z_j}V-\partial^2_{z_iz_j}V)\,e^{-V}\) entering the likelihood \citep{padoan2010,huser2013}. The scale \(\rho_c\) is a variogram distance scale --- at \(d=\rho_c\) the curve sits at \(\chi_{\mathrm{BR},c}\approx0.62\) --- not a storm radius, and not comparable with the parameters named \(\rho\) in the other two models. The model imposes max-stability, hence asymptotic dependence at every finite distance, and a two-parameter shape; where it is misspecified the fit is the best pairwise H\"usler--Reiss approximation.

\subsubsection{Extremal-\texorpdfstring{$t$}{t}}\label{sec:meth-extt}

With the isotropic correlation function and \(T_\nu\) the Student-\(t\) cdf \citep{opitz2013,demarta2005},
\begin{equation}\label{eq:meth-extt-theta}
  r_c(d)=\exp\!\Bigl[-\Bigl(\tfrac{d}{\rho_c}\Bigr)^{\alpha_c}\Bigr],
  \qquad
  \theta_{\mathrm{ExtT},c}(d)=2\,T_{\nu_c+1}\!\Biggl[\Bigl\{\tfrac{(\nu_c+1)(1-r_c(d))}{1+r_c(d)}\Bigr\}^{1/2}\Biggr],
  \qquad
  \chi_{\mathrm{ExtT},c}(d)=2-\theta_{\mathrm{ExtT},c}(d),
\end{equation}
with \(\rho_c>0\), \(\alpha_c\in(0,2]\), \(\nu_c>0\). As \(d\to\infty\), \(r_c(d)\to0\) and \(\chi_{\mathrm{ExtT},c}(d)\to2-2T_{\nu_c+1}(\sqrt{\nu_c+1})>0\), a positive long-range floor set by \(\nu_c\). The bivariate law is \(\exp\{-V^{\mathrm{ExtT}}_d\}\) with
\begin{equation}
  V^{\mathrm{ExtT}}_d(z_i,z_j)
  =\frac{1}{z_i}T_{\nu_c+1}\!\Bigl[\bigl\{(z_j/z_i)^{1/\nu_c}-r_c\bigr\}\bigl(\tfrac{\nu_c+1}{1-r_c^2}\bigr)^{1/2}\Bigr]
  +\frac{1}{z_j}T_{\nu_c+1}\!\Bigl[\bigl\{(z_i/z_j)^{1/\nu_c}-r_c\bigr\}\bigl(\tfrac{\nu_c+1}{1-r_c^2}\bigr)^{1/2}\Bigr],
\end{equation}
\(r_c=r_c(d)\), and density \(f_{\mathrm{ExtT},ij}\) by the same differentiation \citep{opitz2013}. The parameter \(\nu_c\) decouples the overall strength of tail dependence from the rate of decay, so the model can fit curves Brown--Resnick cannot. The positive floor means a fitted long-range \(\chi_{\mathrm{ExtT},c}\) partly reflects the parametric form, and \((\nu_c,\rho_c)\) can be weakly jointly identified, which is why boundary configurations are monitored (Sect.~\ref{sec:meth-cl}) and the comparison is made on \(\chi(d)\) rather than on parameters.

\subsubsection{Spatial Student-\texorpdfstring{$t$}{t} copula}\label{sec:meth-tcop}

On the uniform scale, with the same correlation function,
\begin{equation}
  C_{\mathrm t}\bigl(u_i,u_j;r_c(d),\nu_c\bigr)
  =T_{2,\nu_c}\!\bigl(T^{-1}_{\nu_c}(u_i),T^{-1}_{\nu_c}(u_j);r_c(d)\bigr),
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}\label{eq:meth-tcop-chi}
  \chi_{\mathrm{tCop},c}(d)
  =2\,T_{\nu_c+1}\!\Biggl[-\Bigl\{\tfrac{(\nu_c+1)(1-r_c(d))}{1+r_c(d)}\Bigr\}^{1/2}\Biggr]
  =2-2\,T_{\nu_c+1}\!\Biggl[\Bigl\{\tfrac{(\nu_c+1)(1-r_c(d))}{1+r_c(d)}\Bigr\}^{1/2}\Biggr],
\end{equation}
by symmetry of \(T\), with pairwise density \(c_{\mathrm t}(u_i,u_j;r,\nu)=f_{2,\nu}(T^{-1}_\nu(u_i),T^{-1}_\nu(u_j);r)/\{f_\nu(T^{-1}_\nu(u_i))f_\nu(T^{-1}_\nu(u_j))\}\).

Comparing \eqref{eq:meth-tcop-chi} with \eqref{eq:meth-extt-theta}, the extremal-\(t\) and Student-\(t\) copula share the same bivariate \(\chi(d)\) expression in \((\rho_c,\alpha_c,\nu_c)\), since the extremal-\(t\) process is the max-stable attractor of \(t\) fields \citep{opitz2013,nikoloulopoulos2009}. They differ in model class and likelihood: the extremal-\(t\) is max-stable and fitted to the extreme-value structure on \(\widehat Z\), identifying its parameters from the tail, whereas the copula is fitted to the whole dependence structure on \(\widehat U\), identifying the same-named parameters from the body and extrapolating into the tail. Their fitted curves can therefore differ, and the size of that difference measures how much the conclusion depends on what the likelihood is fitted to. For finite \(\nu_c\), \(\chi_{\mathrm{tCop},c}(d)\to2T_{\nu_c+1}(-\sqrt{\nu_c+1})>0\) as \(r_c(d)\to0\): the copula carries positive tail dependence even at zero correlation, so a fitted long-range \(\chi_{\mathrm{tCop},c}\) is a parametric benchmark rather than evidence of physical dependence between distant storms, and the contrast \(\Delta_{\mathrm{tCop}}^{\chi}(d)\), where the floor partly cancels, is read in preference to either level. The copula is tail-symmetric; only its upper tail is used here.

\subsection{Estimation}\label{sec:meth-cl}

Full \(N\)-variate max-stable likelihoods are intractable, the joint density involving a sum over all partitions of the sites \citep{padoan2010}, so all three methods use pairwise objectives over \(\mathcal P\) and the common days, separately by season. For the two max-stable models,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:meth-cl-ms}
  \ell_{M,c}(\vartheta_{M,c})
  =\sum_{(i,j)\in\mathcal P}\ \sum_{t\in\mathcal T^{ij}_c}
  \log f_{M,ij}\!\bigl(\widehat z_{itc},\widehat z_{jtc};\vartheta_{M,c}\bigr),
  \qquad M\in\{\mathrm{BR},\mathrm{ExtT}\},
\end{equation}
and for the copula, on the uniform scale,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:meth-cl-tcop}
  \ell_{\mathrm{tCop},c}(\vartheta_{\mathrm{tCop},c})
  =\sum_{(i,j)\in\mathcal P}\ \sum_{t\in\mathcal T^{ij}_c}
  \log c_{\mathrm t}\!\bigl(\widehat u_{itc},\widehat u_{jtc};r_c(d_{ij}),\nu_c\bigr),
\end{equation}
where the shared parameter \(\nu_c\) couples all pairs in the two \(t\)-based models. The maximisers \eqref{eq:meth-argmax} give six empirical fits, and each fitted model is turned into its curve and contrast by plug-in,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:meth-output}
  \widehat\chi_{M,c}(d)=\chi_M(d;\widehat\vartheta_{M,c}),
  \qquad
  \widehat\Delta_M^{\chi}(d)=\widehat\chi_{M,\mathrm W}(d)-\widehat\chi_{M,\mathrm S}(d).
\end{equation}

All \(528\) pairs enter with unit weight, a distance truncation being available as a robustness check; the pairwise structure is shared by all three methods so that differences in fitted curves are due to model assumptions rather than to estimator construction, and the use of a pairwise objective for the copula --- where a full likelihood is feasible --- is a deliberate matching choice, at a quantifiable efficiency cost, with a full-likelihood fit kept as a sensitivity check. The constraints are enforced over boxes,
\begin{equation}
  \Theta_{\mathrm{BR}}=[1,3d_{\max}]\times[0.05,2],
  \qquad
  \Theta_{\mathrm{ExtT}}=\Theta_{\mathrm{tCop}}=[1,3d_{\max}]\times[0.05,2]\times[1,50],
\end{equation}
% TODO (code decision): confirm nu bounds [1,50] and the start grids;
% a reduced grid may be needed inside the bootstrap (state it if used,
% and reuse it in the simulation).
maximised by L-BFGS-B from a multi-start grid \(\rho^{(0)}\in\{0.1,0.25,0.5,1,2\}d_{\max}\), \(\alpha^{(0)}\in\{0.5,1,1.5,2\}\), \(\nu^{(0)}\in\{2,5,10,25\}\) where applicable, retaining the best converged optimum. Convergence status and per-coordinate boundary flags \(\{\widehat\vartheta_{M,c}\in\partial\Theta_M\}\) are stored for every fit --- empirical, bootstrap, and simulation --- since estimates piling up at an edge read as weak identification \citep{andrews2001}.

Each pairwise term is a genuine bivariate density, so the composite score is unbiased and \(\widehat\vartheta_{M,c}\) is consistent and asymptotically normal with the Godambe variance \(G^{-1}=H^{-1}JH^{-1}\) \citep{varin2011,padoan2010}; the inverse Hessian is not valid because \(H\ne J\). Serial clustering biases the naive estimate of \(J\) downward, so all reported uncertainty comes from the season-year bootstrap of Sect.~\ref{sec:meth-inference}, with the Godambe form kept only as a reference. Under misspecification, the generic case since at most one model can be correct, \(\widehat\vartheta_{M,c}\) targets the pseudo-true minimiser of the composite divergence and \(\widehat\chi_{M,c}(d)\) is a model-based summary; how far such a summary can sit from a known truth is what Sect.~\ref{sec:simulation} measures.

\subsection{Seasonal comparison}\label{sec:meth-comparison}

On the grid \(\mathcal D=\{d_1,\dots,d_G\}\), the seasonal contrast for each method is
\begin{equation}
  \widehat\Delta_M^{\chi}(d_g)=\widehat\chi_{M,\mathrm W}(d_g)-\widehat\chi_{M,\mathrm S}(d_g),
  \qquad g=1,\dots,G,
\end{equation}
reported with bootstrap bands and summarised at \(\mathcal D_0=\{50,100,200,300\}\)~km. The parameters \(\rho_c\), \(\alpha_c\), and \(\nu_c\) are reported within each model as model-specific quantities, but the comparison across models is made on \(\chi(d)\); no raw \(\rho\) is used as a cross-model summary. Two scalar summaries, defined only as functionals of the curves, accompany the figures,
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{ISD}_M=\frac1G\sum_{g=1}^G\bigl\{\widehat\Delta_M^{\chi}(d_g)\bigr\}^2,
  \qquad
  d^{\mathrm{eff}}_{M,c}=\inf\{d:\chi_{M,c}(d)\le\chi_{M,c}(\infty)+\tfrac12(1-\chi_{M,c}(\infty))\},
\end{equation}
where \(\chi_{\mathrm{BR},c}(\infty)=0\) and \(\chi_{\mathrm{ExtT},c}(\infty)=\chi_{\mathrm{tCop},c}(\infty)=2T_{\nu_c+1}(-\sqrt{\nu_c+1})\); \(\mathrm{ISD}_M\) is reported with the share of \(\mathcal D\) on which \(\widehat\Delta_M^{\chi}>0\), and \(d^{\mathrm{eff}}\) is taken relative to each model's own floor so that it is comparable across models.
% TODO (design): confirm the half-decay constant 1/2; report d_eff only
% when it falls inside [d_min, d_max].

The conclusion is read from the three difference curves jointly. If all three give \(\widehat\Delta_M^{\chi}(d)>0\) with bands clear of zero over \(\mathcal D_0\), the evidence for stronger, more slowly decaying winter dependence is robust to the assumptions separating the methods; if only one does, the finding is model-sensitive; and if they disagree in direction, the disagreement itself localises the assumption --- chiefly whether max-stability is imposed --- on which the conclusion depends. Winter and summer are estimated from disjoint days separated by the excluded transitional seasons and are treated as approximately independent, with residual interannual coupling noted as a limitation.

\subsection{Bootstrap uncertainty}\label{sec:meth-inference}

The pairwise objectives are not genuine likelihoods, storm days cluster in time, and the margins are estimated, so uncertainty is taken from a block bootstrap rather than from a closed-form variance. Resampling individual days would break storm persistence, and resampling individual station--days would in addition break the same-day spatial dependence that is the estimand; the valid unit is the season-year block \(\mathcal B_{c,y}\), a set of whole \(N\)-station days, contiguous within a season and approximately independent across years. The procedure is the following. For \(b=1,\dots,B\):
\begin{enumerate}\setlength{\itemsep}{1pt}
  \item resample \(n_y\) complete season-years with replacement, separately within winter and within summer, giving \(\mathcal Y^{*(b)}_c=(Y^{*(b)}_{c,1},\dots,Y^{*(b)}_{c,n_y})\);
  \item re-estimate the GEV margins on the resample and transform to \(\widehat U^{*(b)}_{\cdot\cdot c}\), \(\widehat Z^{*(b)}_{\cdot\cdot c}\) (fixed-margin variant: reuse the full-data margins);
  \item refit each method, \(\widehat\vartheta^{*(b)}_{M,c}=\arg\max_{\vartheta\in\Theta_M}\ell^{*(b)}_{M,c}(\vartheta)\), recording convergence and boundary flags;
  \item evaluate \(\widehat\chi^{*(b)}_{M,c}(d_g)=\chi_M(d_g;\widehat\vartheta^{*(b)}_{M,c})\) on \(\mathcal D\);
  \item form \(\widehat\Delta^{\chi,*(b)}_M(d_g)=\widehat\chi^{*(b)}_{M,\mathrm W}(d_g)-\widehat\chi^{*(b)}_{M,\mathrm S}(d_g)\).
\end{enumerate}
Pointwise \(95\%\) intervals are the bootstrap percentiles,
\begin{equation}
  CI^{0.95}_M(d_g)=\Bigl[\,q_{0.025}\{\widehat\Delta^{\chi,*}_M(d_g)\},\ q_{0.975}\{\widehat\Delta^{\chi,*}_M(d_g)\}\,\Bigr],
\end{equation}
with the analogous intervals for \(\chi_{M,c}(d_g)\). Algorithm~\ref{alg:meth-bootstrap} states the procedure; all three methods are recomputed on identical resamples, so cross-method differences are bootstrapped jointly.

\begin{algorithm}[t]
  \caption{Season-year block bootstrap (three methods, joint resamples)}
  \label{alg:meth-bootstrap}
  \begin{algorithmic}[1]
    \State \textbf{input:} blocks \(\mathcal B_{c,1},\dots,\mathcal B_{c,n_y}\) for \(c\in\{\mathrm W,\mathrm S\}\); replications \(B\); grid \(\mathcal D\).
    \For{\(b=1,\dots,B\)}
    \For{\(c\in\{\mathrm W,\mathrm S\}\)}
    \State draw \(\mathcal Y^{*(b)}_c\): \(n_y\) season-years with replacement; concatenate intact (days in order, original missingness);
    \State (re-)estimate GEV margins on \(\mathcal Y^{*(b)}_c\); transform to \(\widehat U^{*(b)}_{\cdot\cdot c},\widehat Z^{*(b)}_{\cdot\cdot c}\) \emph{(or reuse full-data margins)};
    \For{\(M\in\{\mathrm{BR},\mathrm{ExtT},\mathrm{tCop}\}\)}
    \State \(\widehat\vartheta^{*(b)}_{M,c}\leftarrow\) maximise \(\ell^{*(b)}_{M,c}\) (multi-start, Sect.~\ref{sec:meth-cl}); store flags; evaluate \(\widehat\chi^{*(b)}_{M,c}(\cdot)\) on \(\mathcal D\);
    \EndFor
    \EndFor
    \State \(\widehat\Delta^{\chi,*(b)}_M(\cdot)\leftarrow\widehat\chi^{*(b)}_{M,\mathrm W}(\cdot)-\widehat\chi^{*(b)}_{M,\mathrm S}(\cdot)\) for each \(M\);
    \EndFor
    \State \textbf{output:} \(CI^{0.95}_M(d_g)\) for \(\Delta_M^{\chi}(d_g)\) and for \(\chi_{M,c}(d_g)\), pointwise over \(\mathcal D\).
  \end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

The intended scheme re-estimates the margins inside the bootstrap, propagating marginal uncertainty into the intervals, with a fixed-margin variant as the fallback.

The primary object is the bootstrap distribution of the difference curve \(\Delta_M^{\chi}(d)\), summarised by the bands \(CI^{0.95}_M\); the directional summary \(\#\{b:\widehat\Delta^{\chi,*(b)}_M(d)>0\}/B\) is reported but is not treated as a \(p\)-value, since no calibration or multiplicity argument is made. At \(n_y\approx35\) blocks the intervals are necessarily coarse, a property the simulation quantifies.

\subsection{Monte Carlo benchmark}\label{sec:meth-simulation}

Because the empirical truth is unknown, the three procedures are benchmarked on data simulated from known data-generating processes on the observed station geometry, under a null in which the two seasons share one curve (\(\Delta_0^{\chi}\equiv0\)) and an alternative in which winter dependence is stronger and decays more slowly (\(\Delta_0^{\chi}>0\)). Every method is fitted to every process, so performance is measured under correct specification and under misspecification, and always on \(\chi(d)\), for which a true value \(\chi_0(d)\) exists in every cell. The design, metrics, and algorithm are in Sect.~\ref{sec:simulation}, and the empirical results of Sect.~\ref{sec:results} are read in their light; because the simulated fields are independent across days, any reported interval coverage is a best case (Sect.~\ref{sec:sim-interp}).

\subsection{Assumptions, in one place}\label{sec:meth-summary}

Table~\ref{tab:meth-models} lists, per model, the scale, parameter vector, curve \(\chi_{M,c}(d)\), likelihood, and main assumption and limitation. The assumptions shared across models are within-season marginal stationarity and GEV upper-tail adequacy (Sect.~\ref{sec:meth-marg-diag}); short-range serial dependence under stationarity and mixing (Sect.~\ref{sec:meth-temporal}); isotropy and within-season spatial stationarity (Sect.~\ref{sec:meth-diagnostics}); and approximate winter--summer independence (Sect.~\ref{sec:meth-comparison}). The design holds everything constant except the dependence assumptions: one standardisation, one curve \(\chi_{M,c}(d)\), matched pairwise estimation, one shared block bootstrap, and one shared benchmark. Section~\ref{sec:simulation} specifies the benchmark, Sect.~\ref{sec:data} the inputs, and Sect.~\ref{sec:results} applies the pipeline in this order.
