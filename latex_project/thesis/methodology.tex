\section{Methodology}\label{sec:methodology}

This section defines the estimator used to compare spatial extremal dependence between winter and summer. The primary object is a season-specific Brown--Resnick max-stable model, fitted by pairwise composite likelihood, and the comparison is between its estimated dependence-range parameters \(\rho_{\mathrm W}\) and \(\rho_{\mathrm S}\). The Brown--Resnick model is used as a parametric approximation to the spatial extremal dependence of daily maximum wind gusts within each season; the daily maxima are not assumed to be exactly max-stable. The nonparametric coefficients that are standard summaries of extremal dependence --- the finite-level tail-dependence coefficient \(\chi_u\), the \(F\)-madogram and the empirical extremal coefficient --- are retained in Section~\ref{sec:meth-diagnostics} as diagnostics for the fitted model rather than as estimands.

Let \(X_{i,t}\) be the daily maximum wind gust at station \(i=1,\dots,N\) on day \(t\), with \(N=33\) KNMI land and coastal stations, each observed on at least \(95\%\) of days over \(1991\)--\(2026\). Station \(i\) is at location \(s_i\in\mathbb R^2\), and \(d_{ij}=\lVert s_i-s_j\rVert\) is the great-circle distance between \(s_i\) and \(s_j\) in kilometres. On day \(t\) the network forms one spatial field
\[
      X_t=\bigl(X_{1,t},\dots,X_{N,t}\bigr),
\]
and the collection of such fields within a season is the input to estimation. The analysis is run separately for winter (DJF) and summer (JJA); \(c\in\{\mathrm W,\mathrm S\}\) indexes the season and \(\mathcal T_c\) the days in season \(c\). December of year \(y\) is assigned to winter \(y+1\), so each winter is contiguous; incomplete boundary winters and the transitional seasons are dropped. For a pair \((i,j)\),
\[
      \mathcal T_c^{ij}=\bigl\{t\in\mathcal T_c:\ X_{i,t}\text{ and }X_{j,t}\text{ both observed}\bigr\},
      \qquad n_{ij}^{(c)}=\bigl|\mathcal T_c^{ij}\bigr|,
\]
and the pairwise likelihood contributions are computed on this pairwise-complete set.

\subsection{Marginal standardisation}\label{sec:meth-marginals}

A max-stable model is specified on a common marginal scale, taken here to be unit Fr\'echet, \(\Pr(Z\le z)=\exp(-1/z)\) for \(z>0\). So that the seasonal comparison reflects spatial dependence and not differences in the local wind climate --- coastal and inland stations differ in their gust distributions, and the two seasons differ as well --- the margins are transformed separately by station \emph{and} season. For station \(i\) and season \(c\) the empirical probability transform is
\[
      \widehat U_{i,t}^{(c)}=\frac{1}{n_i^{(c)}+1}\sum_{r\in\mathcal T_c}\mathbf 1\{X_{i,r}\le X_{i,t}\},
      \qquad t\in\mathcal T_c,
\]
where \(n_i^{(c)}\) is the number of observed daily maxima at station \(i\) in season \(c\); the \((n_i^{(c)}+1)\) denominator keeps the transformed values inside \((0,1)\). The standardised observation on the unit-Fr\'echet scale is
\[
      \widehat Z_{i,t}^{(c)}=-\bigl\{\log\widehat U_{i,t}^{(c)}\bigr\}^{-1}.
\]
The rank transform avoids committing to a parametric marginal model. As a robustness alternative the margins are instead fitted by a generalised extreme value distribution \(\widehat G_i^{(c)}\) and mapped by \(\widehat Z_{i,t}^{(c)}=-1/\log\widehat G_i^{(c)}(X_{i,t})\), which uses the extreme-value marginal limit but is sensitive to the marginal fit. Dependence estimation treats the standardised fields \(\widehat Z_t^{(c)}\) as the data.

\subsection{Brown--Resnick model}\label{sec:meth-br}

For each season let \(Z^{(c)}=\{Z^{(c)}(s):s\in\mathbb R^2\}\) be a stationary max-stable process with unit-Fr\'echet margins. We take \(Z^{(c)}\) to be a Brown--Resnick process \citep{brown1977,kabluchko2009}: its extremal dependence is determined by the variogram of an underlying intrinsically stationary Gaussian process \(W^{(c)}\) with \(W^{(c)}(0)=0\). Writing the variogram as the variance of increments,
\[
      \gamma_c(h)=\operatorname{Var}\!\bigl\{W^{(c)}(s+h)-W^{(c)}(s)\bigr\},
\]
the field is stationary and \(\gamma_c\) governs how fast extremal dependence decays with distance. We do not use the spectral representation directly; estimation uses only the bivariate distributions implied by \(\gamma_c\), given in Section~\ref{sec:meth-cl}. The Brown--Resnick family is used as a parsimonious and interpretable parametric model for the season-specific dependence, not as a literal description of the data-generating mechanism.

\subsection{Variogram and extremal coefficient}\label{sec:meth-variogram}

We use an isotropic powered-exponential variogram,
\[
      \gamma_c(d)=\Bigl(\frac{d}{\rho_c}\Bigr)^{\alpha_c},
      \qquad \rho_c>0,\quad \alpha_c\in(0,2],
\]
so dependence depends on the locations only through the distance \(d\). Here \(\rho_c\) is a range parameter: a larger \(\rho_c\) means slower decay of extremal dependence, hence a larger spatial footprint of extremes; \(\alpha_c\) controls the smoothness of that decay. The pair \(\vartheta_c=(\rho_c,\alpha_c)\) is the parameter of interest.

For two sites at distance \(d\), the Brown--Resnick bivariate distribution function on the unit-Fr\'echet scale is of H\"usler--Reiss type,
\[
      \Pr\!\bigl\{Z^{(c)}(s_i)\le z_i,\,Z^{(c)}(s_j)\le z_j\bigr\}=\exp\!\bigl\{-V_d(z_i,z_j;\vartheta_c)\bigr\},
\]
\[
      V_d(z_i,z_j;\vartheta_c)=\frac{1}{z_i}\,\Phi\!\Bigl(\frac{a_c(d)}{2}+\frac{1}{a_c(d)}\log\frac{z_j}{z_i}\Bigr)
      +\frac{1}{z_j}\,\Phi\!\Bigl(\frac{a_c(d)}{2}+\frac{1}{a_c(d)}\log\frac{z_i}{z_j}\Bigr),
\]
where \(\Phi\) is the standard normal distribution function and
\[
      a_c(d)=\sqrt{\gamma_c(d)}=\Bigl(\frac{d}{\rho_c}\Bigr)^{\alpha_c/2}.
\]
The implied extremal coefficient is \(\theta_c(d)=V_d(1,1;\vartheta_c)\), that is
\[
      \theta_c(d)=2\,\Phi\!\Bigl(\tfrac12 a_c(d)\Bigr)
      =2\,\Phi\!\Bigl(\tfrac12\sqrt{\gamma_c(d)}\Bigr)
      =2\,\Phi\!\Bigl(\tfrac12 (d/\rho_c)^{\alpha_c/2}\Bigr)\in[1,2],
\]
decreasing from \(\theta_c(0)=1\) (complete dependence) to \(2\) (independence).

\paragraph{Convention.} Throughout, \(\gamma_c\) is the variogram in the sense of the variance of increments. Some references state the same model through the semivariogram \(\tilde\gamma_c=\gamma_c/2\), for which the extremal coefficient reads \(\theta_c(d)=2\Phi\!\bigl(\sqrt{\tilde\gamma_c(d)/2}\bigr)\); the two expressions are identical. The convention \(a_c(d)=\sqrt{\gamma_c(d)}\), \(\theta_c(d)=2\Phi(a_c(d)/2)\) is used in the simulation of Section~\ref{sec:simulation} and in the implementation.

\subsection{Pairwise composite likelihood}\label{sec:meth-cl}

Full-likelihood inference for max-stable processes is infeasible beyond a few sites, because the joint density involves a sum over all partitions of the locations \citep{padoan2010}. We therefore estimate \(\vartheta_c\) by pairwise composite likelihood, replacing the joint density by a product of bivariate densities. The bivariate density of a pair at distance \(d\) is obtained from \(V_d\) by differentiation,
\[
      f_d(z_i,z_j;\vartheta_c)=\Bigl\{\partial_{z_i}V_d\,\partial_{z_j}V_d-\partial^2_{z_i z_j}V_d\Bigr\}\exp\!\bigl\{-V_d(z_i,z_j;\vartheta_c)\bigr\},
\]
whose closed form for the H\"usler--Reiss exponent is given by \citet{padoan2010} and, for the Brown--Resnick process specifically, by \citet{huser2013}. The pairwise composite log-likelihood for season \(c\) is
\[
      \ell_c(\vartheta_c)=\sum_{i<j} w_{ij}\sum_{t\in\mathcal T_c^{ij}}
      \log f_{d_{ij}}\!\bigl(\widehat Z_{i,t}^{(c)},\widehat Z_{j,t}^{(c)};\vartheta_c\bigr),
\]
and the estimator is
\[
      \widehat\vartheta_c=(\widehat\rho_c,\widehat\alpha_c)=\arg\max_{\vartheta_c\in\Theta}\ \ell_c(\vartheta_c),
      \qquad \Theta=[\rho_L,\rho_U]\times[\alpha_L,2].
\]
The weights \(w_{ij}\in\{0,1\}\) restrict the sum to pairs with \(d_{ij}\le r\) for a cut-off \(r\); distant pairs are close to independent and carry little dependence information, and truncating them is the standard weighting in pairwise likelihood for spatial extremes \citep{padoan2010}. The cut-off \(r\), the parameter bounds, and the choice \(w_{ij}\equiv1\) versus a finite \(r\) are reported with the estimates and varied as a robustness check. As a further robustness alternative the pairwise likelihood may be censored below a high marginal threshold, so that only joint tail observations inform the fit \citep{huser2013}; if this is used empirically, the simulation of Section~\ref{sec:simulation} is re-run with the same censoring. Each day contributes one term per retained pair. Consecutive daily maxima within a multi-day storm are not independent; under stationarity and standard mixing conditions this serial dependence bears mainly on the sampling variability of \(\widehat\vartheta_c\), and hence on inference, which is handled by the bootstrap of Section~\ref{sec:meth-inference}.

\subsection{Seasonal comparison and hypotheses}\label{sec:meth-comparison}

The model is estimated separately for the two seasons, giving \(\widehat\vartheta_{\mathrm W}=(\widehat\rho_{\mathrm W},\widehat\alpha_{\mathrm W})\) and \(\widehat\vartheta_{\mathrm S}=(\widehat\rho_{\mathrm S},\widehat\alpha_{\mathrm S})\); both the range and the smoothness are estimated freely in each season. The primary comparison is of the dependence ranges through
\[
      \widehat\Delta_\rho=\widehat\rho_{\mathrm W}-\widehat\rho_{\mathrm S}.
\]
Winter extremes are expected to have a larger spatial footprint than summer convective extremes, which under this model corresponds to a larger winter range. The hypotheses are
\[
      H_0:\ \rho_{\mathrm W}=\rho_{\mathrm S}
      \qquad\text{against}\qquad
      H_1:\ \rho_{\mathrm W}>\rho_{\mathrm S}.
\]
Because winter and summer are estimated from disjoint days, \(\widehat\rho_{\mathrm W}\) and \(\widehat\rho_{\mathrm S}\) are computed on independent samples. The comparison is also read off the implied extremal-coefficient curves \(\widehat\theta_{\mathrm W}(d)\) and \(\widehat\theta_{\mathrm S}(d)\), which place the parameter difference on the dependence-versus-distance scale and are directly comparable to the diagnostics of Section~\ref{sec:meth-diagnostics}. The smoothness parameters are compared secondarily through \(\widehat\alpha_{\mathrm W}-\widehat\alpha_{\mathrm S}\); \(\rho\) is the object of interest.

\subsection{Inference}\label{sec:meth-inference}

Uncertainty is quantified primarily by a seasonal-year block bootstrap. The resampling unit is one season-year: within each season the season-years are resampled with replacement, independently across winter and summer, and for each replication the marginal transform of Section~\ref{sec:meth-marginals} and the estimator of Section~\ref{sec:meth-cl} are recomputed, yielding \(\widehat\vartheta_{\mathrm W}^{*}\), \(\widehat\vartheta_{\mathrm S}^{*}\) and \(\widehat\Delta_\rho^{*}\). Resampling whole season-years rather than individual days preserves the within-storm serial dependence between consecutive daily maxima and the cross-station dependence among pairs that share a station, both of which a day-level resample would destroy. Percentile intervals for \(\rho_c\), \(\alpha_c\) and \(\Delta_\rho\), and pointwise bands for \(\theta_c(d)\), are formed over \(B\) replications. The test of \(H_0\) against \(H_1\) is an interval-based one-sided bootstrap test: it rejects \(H_0\) when the one-sided bootstrap percentile interval for \(\widehat\Delta_\rho\) excludes zero, equivalently when the lower percentile bound of \(\widehat\Delta_\rho\) lies above zero, with the corresponding one-sided bootstrap \(p\)-value reported. With on the order of \(35\) season-years per season the bands are correspondingly coarse, which the simulation of Section~\ref{sec:simulation} quantifies.

As a parametric cross-check under the working assumption of independent replicates, composite-likelihood theory gives the Godambe (sandwich) information \(G(\vartheta_c)=H(\vartheta_c)\,J(\vartheta_c)^{-1}H(\vartheta_c)\), where \(H\) is the expected negative Hessian of \(\ell_c\) and \(J\) the variance of its score, and \(\widehat\vartheta_c\) is asymptotically normal with covariance \(G(\vartheta_c)^{-1}\) \citep{padoan2010}. Ordinary likelihood standard errors do not apply, because \(\ell_c\) is a composite and not a genuine log-likelihood. Since the independence assumption underlying \(J\) is violated by serial dependence within storms, the sandwich errors are reported only alongside the block bootstrap, which is the basis for the reported intervals and the test.

\subsection{Nonparametric diagnostics}\label{sec:meth-diagnostics}

The fitted model is assessed against three nonparametric pairwise summaries computed from the same standardised fields, none of which is the estimand. The first is the finite-level tail-dependence coefficient at level \(u\),
\[
      \widehat\chi_{u,ij}^{(c)}=\frac{\#\{t\in\mathcal T_c^{ij}:\widehat U_{i,t}^{(c)}>u,\ \widehat U_{j,t}^{(c)}>u\}}
      {\#\{t\in\mathcal T_c^{ij}:\widehat U_{i,t}^{(c)}>u\}},
\]
the empirical probability that station \(j\) is extreme given that station \(i\) is, reported on the grid \(u\in\{0.95,0.975,0.99\}\) and symmetrised over the two conditioning directions \citep{coles1999}. The second is the \(F\)-madogram and its extremal coefficient \citep{cooley2006},
\[
      \widehat\nu_{F,ij}^{(c)}=\frac{1}{2n_{ij}^{(c)}}\sum_{t\in\mathcal T_c^{ij}}\bigl|\widehat U_{i,t}^{(c)}-\widehat U_{j,t}^{(c)}\bigr|,
      \qquad
      \widehat\theta_{ij}^{(c)}=\frac{1+2\,\widehat\nu_{F,ij}^{(c)}}{1-2\,\widehat\nu_{F,ij}^{(c)}}\in[1,2].
\]
Under a Brown--Resnick model the implied finite-level coefficient is \(\chi_u^{\mathrm{BR}}(d)=(1-2u+u^{\theta_c(d)})/(1-u)\), and the implied extremal coefficient is \(\theta_c(d)\). The diagnostics check whether the fitted \(\widehat\theta_c(\cdot)\) and the implied \(\chi_u^{\mathrm{BR}}(\cdot)\) track the pairwise estimates \((d_{ij},\widehat\theta_{ij}^{(c)})\) and \((d_{ij},\widehat\chi_{u,ij}^{(c)})\) across distance. Systematic departure --- for instance pairwise \(\widehat\chi_{u,ij}^{(c)}\) decaying towards zero at the highest level while the fitted curve stays bounded away from it --- indicates that the asymptotic-dependence assumption of the Brown--Resnick model is questionable for these data \citep{davison2013}. Pairwise estimates and the fitted curve are always shown together, so the comparison is visible rather than smoothed away. The diagnostics use scalar distance and therefore assume isotropy; departures associated with prevailing storm direction are examined as a robustness check.

The finite-sample behaviour of \(\widehat\vartheta_c\) and of the test of \(H_0\) is evaluated in Section~\ref{sec:simulation}.
