\section{Literature Review}\label{sec:literature}

The thesis sits at the intersection of univariate extreme value
theory, spatial extremes, and the applied literature on wind gust
extremes. This section reviews the strands that motivate the
methodological choices in Sections~\ref{sec:evt-spatial} and
\ref{sec:methodology}. We organise the review by methodological
role: only papers that supply a definition, an estimator, an
empirical benchmark, or a non-stationarity hook for the seasonal
question are discussed.

\subsection{Extreme value theory for environmental
data}\label{sec:lit-univariate}

The point of departure is the classical extreme value theorem and
its threshold counterpart. Under mild conditions, the suitably
normalised maximum of an i.i.d.\ sequence converges to the
generalised extreme value (GEV) distribution, and the conditional
exceedance of a continuous variable over a high threshold converges
to the generalised Pareto (GPD) family. Both limits underlie the
marginal modelling of \(\mathrm{FX}\) at each station before any
dependence object is estimated. Textbook-level treatments include
the monograph by \citet{TODO-falk2019} on multivariate extreme value
theory and \(D\)-norms, which we use as a background reference for
the joint representation results that recur in
Section~\ref{sec:evt-spatial}.

The applied wind-extremes literature has used both routes. In a
direct precedent for our marginal step, \citet{TODO-brabson2000}
fit the GPD to peak gust speeds at five Scottish sites and study
how the shape parameter \(\xi\) varies with the threshold choice;
their conclusion that GPD predictions are sensitive to threshold
selection motivates our robustness check over multiple threshold
quantiles in Section~\ref{sec:rob-extremes}. Earlier directional
GEV work by \citet{TODO-coles1994} treats extreme wind speeds as a
function of wind direction and is one of the first papers to import
spatial-extremes techniques into a wind context; we cite it as a
possible robustness extension if seasonal differences turn out to
covary with prevailing wind direction.

A practical complication for hourly wind data is serial dependence:
consecutive hourly observations during a single storm cannot be
treated as independent. \citet{TODO-fawcett2006} model this through
a Markov chain on the high-threshold exceedance process and argue
that ignoring within-storm dependence biases standard errors of
extreme-quantile estimates. \citet{TODO-ferro2003} provide an
automatic declustering scheme based on the extremal index that can
be applied before any inference about cluster characteristics. We
adopt this scheme as the standard pre-processing step on the
peaks-over-threshold route in Section~\ref{sec:meth-extremes}.

\subsection{Spatial extremes and max-stable
processes}\label{sec:lit-spatial}

The natural generalisation of univariate extreme value theory to a
spatial field is the class of max-stable processes, which arise as
the limit of pointwise maxima of i.i.d.\ replicated fields. They
provide closed-form expressions for the joint exceedance
probabilities of a finite collection of locations and form the
theoretical motivation for the pairwise dependence summaries used
in this thesis.

The foundational practical reference is
\citet{TODO-dehaan2006}, who construct simple stationary
max-stable processes in \(\mathbb{R}\) and \(\mathbb{R}^2\) with a
single dependence parameter and a closed-form decay of pairwise
tail dependence with distance, and prove consistency and asymptotic
normality of two semiparametric estimators of that parameter.
\citet{TODO-davison2012} review the latent-variable, copula, and
max-stable routes to spatial extremes and compare them on summer
maximum daily rainfall at 51 Swiss stations; their result that
max-stable models and copula models capture the joint upper tail
substantially better than latent-variable models is the
methodological reason we work directly with pairwise dependence
objects. The inferential engine for max-stable models in moderate
dimensions is the pairwise composite likelihood of
\citet{TODO-padoan2010}, which replaces the intractable
\(D\)-dimensional density by a sum of pair-level densities and
estimates marginal and dependence parameters jointly. We treat
this route as optional rather than central
(Section~\ref{sec:meth-maxstable}); the headline empirical
analysis uses non-parametric pairwise summaries.

A more recent review, \citet{TODO-huser2020}, surveys what has
been added since 2012: \(r\)-Pareto processes for spatial
threshold exceedances, conditional spatial extremes models, and
random-scale-mixture constructions that interpolate between
asymptotic dependence and asymptotic independence. Their main
methodological message is that classical max-stable models often
impose more asymptotic dependence than data exhibit. We honour
this caveat by reporting empirical \(\widehat{\chi}^{(c)}_u\) on a
grid of \(u\) rather than at a single tail level.

\subsection{Pairwise extremal dependence
measures}\label{sec:lit-pairwise}

The pairwise summaries on which the empirical part of this thesis
relies — the bivariate extremal coefficient \(\theta(s_i,s_j)\in
[1,2]\), the upper tail dependence coefficient \(\chi(s_i,s_j)\in
[0,1]\), and the chibar coefficient \(\bar\chi\) used to diagnose
asymptotic independence — are surveyed by
\citet{TODO-cooley2012}. They lay out the empirical estimators
(F-madogram for \(\theta\); empirical conditional tail probabilities
for \(\chi\) and \(\bar\chi\)) and the conditions under which each
is well defined. We follow their notation closely in
Section~\ref{sec:evt-spatial}.

A central caveat for any empirical application is delivered by
\citet{TODO-wadsworth2012}, who introduce a flexible model class
that interpolates between asymptotic dependence and asymptotic
independence and apply it to significant wave heights in the North
Sea. They show that finite-level pairwise dependence can be much
weaker than the asymptotic dependence implied by a fitted
max-stable model, and recommend reporting the level-dependent
\(\chi(u) = \Pr(U_j > u \mid U_i > u)\) rather than committing to a
single asymptotic value. This is directly relevant to our seasonal
comparison: summer convective gust events may have a finite-level
spatial signature that vanishes at the very top of the tail, so we
report \(\widehat{\chi}^{(c)}_u\) on a grid of \(u\) and interpret
the seasonal contrast at finite tail levels.

A functional-data analogue of the extremal correlation coefficient
is developed by \citet{TODO-kim2024}; it falls outside the scope
of the scalar-FX thesis but is recorded as a future-work pointer
in Section~\ref{sec:conclusion-future}. Vine copulas
\citep{TODO-aas2008} are likewise out of scope as the main
dependence object; they are mentioned only as the natural starting
point if a future extension wants to model the joint upper tail of
\(\mathrm{FX}\) across more than two stations at once.

\subsection{Wind gust and windstorm
extremes}\label{sec:lit-wind}

The closest empirical predecessor to this thesis is
\citet{TODO-bonazzi2012}, who use bivariate extreme value copulas
to characterise the spatial dependence of peak gusts across 135
major European windstorms over 1972--2010. Their data combine
several national networks, including 34 Dutch KNMI stations, and
they show that pairwise dependence varies substantially between
storms and across climate-variability regimes. The present thesis
differs by (i) working at the seasonal rather than the per-storm
level, (ii) using only the KNMI network with hourly maximum wind
gusts (\(\mathrm{FX}\)), and (iii) summarising dependence through
the extremal coefficient and tail dependence coefficient rather
than through Pickands' dependence function. Bonazzi
et~al.'s estimates provide a useful empirical order of magnitude
against which the winter dependence-distance curves of
Section~\ref{sec:results-distance} can be compared.

European windstorm climatology more broadly is treated by
\citet{TODO-dellamarta2009}, who apply peak-over-threshold analysis
to ERA-40 reanalysis wind gust/speed fields to estimate return
periods for named European storms, and by
\citet{TODO-roberts2014}, who curate the XWS catalogue of 50
extreme European windstorms over 1979--2012 with footprint maps.
Both provide empirical context for what should drive the winter
upper tail of \(\mathrm{FX}\) at Dutch stations, and the XWS
catalogue is the natural cross-reference for sanity-checking the
events that dominate the seasonal pairwise dependence in winter.
\citet{TODO-klawa2003} construct a station-level storm-loss index
in Germany by scaling daily peak gusts by the local upper 2\%
quantile; we cite it as a methodological precedent for working
with locally normalised gust exceedances.

\subsection{Seasonal and nonstationary
extremes}\label{sec:lit-seasonal}

The seasonal question undertaken in this thesis is, formally, a
question about non-stationary dependence: the spatial extremal
dependence structure is allowed to depend on a covariate, here the
seasonal label \(c \in \{\mathrm{W}, \mathrm{S}\}\). A visual
precedent for the seasonal contrast is already in the Swiss
rainfall application of \citet{TODO-davison2012}: their Figure~2
displays annual, summer (June--August) and winter (December--
February) maximum daily rainfall at four station pairs and shows
that the joint behaviour differs visibly across seasons even on a
small panel. The formal counterpart that allows the dependence
structure itself to depend on a covariate is provided by
\citet{TODO-huser-genton2016}.
They construct max-stable models in which the dependence kernel
itself is a function of covariates through a Paciorek--Schervish
construction, fit them by pairwise composite likelihood, and show
through simulation that ignoring non-stationarity in the dependence
structure can substantially bias return-level estimates for
spatial functionals. The present thesis does not fit a parametric
non-stationary model; instead it estimates separate dependence
structures for winter and summer and asks empirically whether the
two differ. Huser and Genton supply the theoretical justification
that this question is well posed.

The case for stratifying by season rather than fitting a single
non-stationary annual model is methodological: stratification
sidesteps the strong within-year non-stationarity in the joint
distribution of hourly wind gusts by restricting attention to
windows over which a within-season stationarity assumption is at
least more defensible. The cost is a smaller effective sample per
season and a residual within-season non-stationarity that is
treated as part of the robustness analysis
(Section~\ref{sec:rob-period}).

Functional-extreme-value approaches such as the heat-wave
construction of \citet{TODO-french2018} and the functional
peaks-over-threshold framework of
\citet{TODO-fondeville2022} are noted as future-work directions
for representing storm events as intraday curves rather than as
hourly maxima; they are outside the scope of the scalar-FX
thesis. Likewise the functional-extreme tooling developed in
\citet{TODO-kim2019,TODO-kokoszka2017,TODO-kokoszka2019} is
recorded only as a future-work pointer.

\subsection{Positioning of this thesis}\label{sec:lit-positioning}

The thesis combines four ingredients from the literature reviewed
above. From \citet{TODO-cooley2012} and
\citet{TODO-davison2012} we take the pairwise dependence summaries
\(\theta\) and \(\chi\) and the F-madogram / empirical conditional
estimators. From \citet{TODO-dehaan2006} we take the view that
spatial extremal dependence is naturally summarised as a function
of pairwise distance. From \citet{TODO-wadsworth2012} we take the
discipline of reporting \(\chi(u)\) on a grid rather than at a
single asymptotic level. From \citet{TODO-huser-genton2016} we
take the formal warrant for treating the seasonal label as a
covariate that indexes a family of dependence structures.

The empirical hook is the windstorm literature
(\citealt{TODO-bonazzi2012}, \citealt{TODO-dellamarta2009},
\citealt{TODO-roberts2014}, \citealt{TODO-klawa2003}). The
contribution is to apply this combination of tools to a single
homogeneous national network — the KNMI hourly archive — and to
compare the resulting dependence-distance summary explicitly
between winter and summer. We do not attempt a parametric
non-stationary max-stable fit; we do not extend to compound hazards
or vine copulas as the main method; and we do not draw climate-
change conclusions.
