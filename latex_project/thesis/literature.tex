\section{Literature Review}\label{sec:literature}

This paper draws on three strands of literature: univariate extreme-value theory, spatial extremes, and empirical work on European windstorms. Section~\ref{sec:lit-evt-intro} discusses the extreme-value foundations used to define station-level extremes. Section~\ref{sec:lit-spatial-extremes} reviews spatial extremes and max-stable processes, which are properties needed for the spatial analysis. Section~\ref{sec:lit-pairwise-dependence} focuses on pairwise extremal-dependence measures, which form the main theoretical objects for this paper. Section~\ref{sec:lit-windstorms} discusses windstorm applications that motivate the seasonal comparison. Section~\ref{sec:lit-nonstationarity} positions the seasonal split as a restricted form of non-stationary extremal dependence. Section~\ref{sec:lit-finite-sample-inference} discusses the finite-sample inference problem that arises from estimating pairwise dependence for station pairs in a finite sample setting. Finally, Section~\ref{sec:lit-positioning} summarises how the literature is used in this paper and how the paper contributes to the existing corpus of literature.
% =====================================================================

Since this paper studies whether extreme wind gusts observed at KNMI station locations are more spatially dependent in winter than in summer. The first part introduces the extreme-value theory, for modelling tail observations. The second part reviews max-stable processes and pairwise likelihood methods, which provide the standard framework for spatial block maxima. The third part discusses pairwise dependence summaries, in particular extremal coefficients, madogram estimators, finite-level tail probabilities, and extremograms. The final part positions the seasonal comparison as a form of non-stationary extremal dependence.

\subsection{Extreme-value foundations}\label{sec:lit-evt-foundations}

Extreme-value theory provides the asymptotic basis for modelling rare events rather than average behaviour. Let \(X_1,\ldots,X_n\) denote independent observations with common distribution function \(F\), and let \(M_n=\max\{X_1,\ldots,X_n\}\). Then
\[
    \Pr(M_n \leq z)
    =
    \Pr(X_1\leq z,\ldots,X_n\leq z)
    =
    F(z)^n .
\]
The problem to tackle is that the distribution of \(M_n\) degenerates as \(n\) increases unless appropriate normalising constants are used. The results of \citet{fisher1928} and \citet{gnedenko1943} show that, if normalised maxima converge to a non-degenerate limit, the limiting distribution must belong to the generalized extreme-value family. The univariate theory is developed extensively in \citet{dehaanferreira2006}, including the connection between marginal extremes and multivariate or spatial extensions.
In order to obtain the extreme values from the data, two main approaches are commonly used in literature. The first is the block-maxima approach, in which the sample is divided into non-overlapping blocks and only the maximum observation within each block is retained. In our paper, a block may be defined by season and year, so that the winter maximum at one station is compared with the corresponding winter maxima at the other stations. The advantage of this approach is that it is directly connected to the limiting theory for maxima: under suitable normalisation, block maxima are approximately generalized extreme-value distributed \citep{fisher1928,gnedenko1943,dehaanferreira2006}. In the spatial case, taking pointwise maxima over a random field leads to max-stable processes as the natural limiting objects \citep{dehaan1984,smith1990,dehaanferreira2006}. Extremal coefficients and related pairwise summaries are most naturally defined within the max-stable framework, which will be discussed  section~\ref{sec:lit-maxstable}. The cost of this approach is that block maxima discard information. High wind gusts that occur within the same season but are not the seasonal maximum do not enter the analysis. The block-maxima approach also requires assumptions. In the classical formulation, the observations are independent and identically distributed, or at least satisfy weak-dependence conditions that allow maxima to have the same type of limiting behaviour as in the independent case \citep{leadbetter1983,dehaanferreira2006}. Wind gusts are serially dependent, as several large observations may belong to the same storm system. Taking seasonal maxima reduces the direct influence storm clustering, but it does not remove all dependence or all forms of non-stationarity. The assumption is therefore that, after conditioning on season and after marginal standardisation, the seasonal block maxima are sufficiently close to their limiting extreme-value approximation as the number of seasons increases.

The second approach to obtain the extreme values, is the peaks-over-threshold approach. Instead of keeping only the maximum within each block, this method retains all observations exceeding a high threshold. The asymptotic justification is given by the Pickands--Balkema--de Haan theorem, according to which exceedances above a sufficiently high threshold can be approximated by a generalized Pareto distribution \citep{balkema1974,pickands1975,coles2001}. This approach uses more tail information than block maxima. Its difficulty is that the threshold must be chosen in finite samples, while the theory concerns the limit as the threshold increases. Moreover, exceedances of wind gusts are often temporally clustered during the same storm, so declustering or an explicit treatment of temporal dependence is needed if exceedances are interpreted as separate extreme events \citep{leadbetter1983,coles2001,ferro2003}. Since the peak-over-threshold is not considering the seasonal structure in its plain vanilla form, it is less interpretable for the seasonal comparison, and requires adjustments. 

\subsection{Spatial max-stable models and pairwise inference}\label{sec:lit-maxstable}

Max-stable processes extend the block-maxima logic to spatial data. If independent replications of a spatial random field are observed and pointwise maxima are taken over blocks, then max-stable processes arise as the natural limiting objects, in the same way that generalized extreme-value distributions arise for scalar maxima. \citet{smith1990} introduced one of the earliest explicit spatial max-stable models, motivated by windstorm profiles. \citet{schlather2002} developed stationary max-stable random fields based on Gaussian processes, giving a practically useful class of models in which extremal dependence is controlled through an underlying covariance function. \citet{kabluchko2009} later developed the Brown--Resnick class through Gaussian processes with stationary increments. In this model class, dependence is governed by the variogram of the underlying Gaussian process, so that the strength of extremal dependence can be related directly to spatial separation.

The variogram is therefore useful in our study. In classical geostatistics, the semivariogram is written as
[
\gamma(h)
=
\frac{1}{2}\operatorname{Var}{W(s+h)-W(s)},
]
where (W) is a spatial process and (h) is a spatial lag \citep{matheron1963,cressie1993}. For Brown--Resnick max-stable processes, the variogram does not enter as a descriptive tool for ordinary spatial interpolation, but as a model component that controls how extremal dependence decays with distance \citep{kabluchko2009,davison2012}. A slower increase of (\gamma(h)) corresponds to stronger dependence over longer distances, while a faster increase corresponds to more localised extremes. This is relevant for the simulation part of our paper: a winter setting with stronger spatial extremal dependence can be represented by a variogram that increases more slowly with distance than the corresponding summer setting. The empirical analysis itself remains based on pairwise extremal-dependence summaries, rather than on variogram estimation as the main object of interest.

A practical difficulty is that max-stable processes are hard to estimate in moderate or high dimension. The full multivariate density is usually unavailable or computationally impractical once many spatial locations are observed. \citet{padoan2010} address this issue by using pairwise composite likelihood methods for max-stable processes. This establishes pairwise likelihood as a standard compromise in spatial extremes: it gives up the full likelihood, but retains enough bivariate information to estimate dependence parameters. \citet{davison2012} review this literature and compare latent variable models, copula models, and max-stable models for spatial extremes, making clear that successful modelling of marginal extremes does not by itself imply successful modelling of joint tail behaviour.

Our paper uses this pairwise logic, but does not make full parametric max-stable estimation the main empirical object. The aim is more limited. Pairwise dependence summaries are estimated separately for winter and summer and then compared across inter-station distance. This keeps the empirical interpretation close to the research question: if one station observes an extreme wind gust, how likely is another station to be extreme as well?

\subsection{Pairwise measures of extremal spatial dependence}\label{sec:lit-pairwise-measures}

The empirical analysis requires dependence measures that can be interpreted at the station-pair level. A common summary is the pairwise extremal coefficient. For a max-stable process \(Z(s)\) with unit Frechet margins, the pairwise extremal coefficient \(\theta(s_i,s_j)\) may be defined through
\[
    \Pr\{Z(s_i)\leq z, Z(s_j)\leq z\}
    =
    \exp\left\{-\frac{\theta(s_i,s_j)}{z}\right\},
    \qquad z>0 .
\]
The coefficient satisfies \(1\leq \theta(s_i,s_j)\leq 2\). Values close to one correspond to strong extremal dependence, while values close to two correspond to weak extremal dependence. In the seasonal comparison considered here, stronger winter dependence corresponds to lower winter values of \(\theta(s_i,s_j)\) than summer values at comparable distances.

\citet{schlather2003} study dependence measures for multivariate and spatial extremes and provide a formal basis for using extremal coefficients as dependence summaries. For the present application, the attraction of \(\theta\) is not only theoretical. It also gives a simple interpretation when plotted against distance: it describes how quickly extremal dependence weakens as station pairs are further apart.

An empirical route to \(\theta\) is provided by the \(F\)-madogram. \citet{cooley2006} study variogram-type summaries for spatial max-stable random fields and show how the madogram can be used to estimate extremal dependence. For transformed margins, the \(F\)-madogram between two locations is
\[
    \nu_F(s_i,s_j)
    =
    \frac{1}{2}
    \mathbb{E}
    \left[
        \left|F_{s_i}\{X(s_i)\}-F_{s_j}\{X(s_j)\}\right|
    \right],
\]
where \(F_{s_i}\) and \(F_{s_j}\) are the marginal distribution functions at the two locations. Under bivariate max-stability, the corresponding extremal coefficient is
\[
    \theta(s_i,s_j)
    =
    \frac{1+2\nu_F(s_i,s_j)}{1-2\nu_F(s_i,s_j)} .
\]
This estimator is especially useful in this thesis because it is nonparametric after marginal standardisation and can be computed for many station pairs. The resulting estimates can then be smoothed or grouped by distance to obtain winter and summer dependence--distance curves.

A second summary is the upper-tail dependence coefficient. Let \(U(s_i)\) and \(U(s_j)\) denote marginally standardised observations on the uniform scale. The finite-level conditional tail probability is
\[
    \chi_u(s_i,s_j)
    =
    \Pr\{U(s_j)>u \mid U(s_i)>u\},
\]
for a high threshold \(u\). The corresponding asymptotic coefficient is
\[
    \chi(s_i,s_j)
    =
    \lim_{u\uparrow 1}\chi_u(s_i,s_j),
\]
provided the limit exists. The finite-level version is close to the empirical question of the thesis, because it directly asks how often one station is extreme conditional on another station being extreme. It is also more cautious in finite samples, since the number of seasonal extremes is limited and the limiting approximation may not be accurate at the thresholds observed in practice.

The extremogram provides a related way to describe extremal dependence. \citet{davis2009} introduce the extremogram as an analogue of the autocorrelation function for extreme events in stationary time series. \citet{cho2016} extend this idea to the spatial setting and study the empirical spatial extremogram under lattice and continuous-domain sampling designs. Their formulation is relevant for KNMI station data because it treats extremal dependence as a function of spatial lag or distance. In this thesis, the spatial extremogram provides methodological support for estimating tail dependence across station pairs and summarising it as a distance curve, rather than focusing on isolated bivariate comparisons.

\subsection{Seasonal variation as non-stationary extremal dependence}\label{sec:lit-seasonal-nonstationary}

The winter--summer comparison can be interpreted as a restricted form of non-stationary spatial extremal dependence. A fully stationary spatial extremes model would impose the same dependence structure throughout the year. That assumption is difficult to defend for Dutch wind gusts, since winter wind extremes are often associated with large-scale synoptic systems, while summer gust extremes may be more localised. The seasonal split is a simple way to relax annual stationarity without fitting a high-dimensional non-stationary model.

\citet{huser2016} develop non-stationary dependence structures for spatial extremes in which covariates can enter the dependence model itself. Their framework is more general than the one used here, but it supports the main modelling idea: extremal dependence need not be constant over covariates or regimes. In the present thesis, season is treated as a regime variable. Instead of estimating a single annual dependence curve, the pairwise summaries are estimated separately for DJF and JJA. This sacrifices parametric efficiency, but it makes the interpretation transparent and reduces reliance on a single non-stationary max-stable specification.

A separate issue is that asymptotic models may be too rigid at observed levels. \citet{wadsworth2012} discuss spatial extremes in settings where data may show dependence at observable levels while becoming independent in the limit. \citet{huser2022} provide a broader review of recent spatial-extremes methodology and emphasise that classical max-stable models can be too restrictive for finite environmental data, especially when dependence weakens at more severe levels. This is relevant for the empirical design because the seasonal comparison should not depend only on an asymptotic model class. For this reason, the thesis considers both extremal-coefficient estimates and finite-level conditional tail probabilities.

\subsection{Positioning of this thesis}\label{sec:lit-positioning}

The methodological literature gives the thesis a narrow empirical role. Extreme-value theory justifies modelling tail behaviour separately from average wind behaviour. Spatial max-stable processes provide the standard limiting framework for block maxima over space. Pairwise likelihood methods explain why bivariate information is commonly used in spatial extremes when full likelihood methods are infeasible. Madogram estimators, extremal coefficients, finite-level \(\chi_u\) probabilities, and spatial extremograms then provide interpretable station-pair summaries.

This thesis does not propose a new spatial-extremes model. It studies whether existing pairwise summaries can recover and describe seasonal differences in spatial extremal dependence of Dutch wind gusts. The simulation study evaluates the finite-sample behaviour of these summaries under controlled dependence structures. The empirical analysis applies the same procedure to KNMI wind-gust observations and compares winter and summer dependence--distance curves. The contribution is therefore methodological as well as empirical: the thesis examines whether a transparent pairwise framework can be used to detect whether winter wind-gust extremes are more spatially widespread than summer wind-gust extremes.
