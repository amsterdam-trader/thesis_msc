\section{Literature Review}\label{sec:literature}

This section reviews the literature on extreme-value theory for environmental data, spatial extremes and max-stable processes, pairwise extremal-dependence measures, data-generating processes for the simulation study, inference and uncertainty for dependence estimators, and seasonal and non-stationary extremal dependence.

\subsection{Extreme-value theory}\label{sec:lit-evt}

Extreme-value theory provides the statistical framework for studying rare events in the tails of a distribution. For the canonical case of maxima, let $X_1,\ldots,X_n$ be independent observations with common distribution function $F$ and let $M_n=\max\{X_1,\ldots,X_n\}$. Then
\[
    \Pr\{M_n \leq z\}
    = \Pr\{X_1 \leq z,\ldots,X_n \leq z\}
    = F(z)^n .
\]
The results of \citet{fisher1928} and \citet{gnedenko1943} show that, under normalisation, any non-degenerate limit of normalised sample maxima must belong to the generalised extreme value (GEV) family, of which the Gumbel, Fr\'echet, and Weibull types are special cases. This justifies modelling tail observations directly and provides the basis for the block-maxima approach, in which the data are divided into non-overlapping blocks and the maximum within each block is modelled. Block maxima are also the entry point for spatial extremes, since spatial max-stable processes arise as the limiting objects for spatial block maxima \citep{dehaan2006}.

In our paper the blocks are days: the hourly observations are aggregated to daily maximum wind gusts at each station, and these are the data object for all subsequent dependence estimation. Daily maxima sit between two extremes of block length. Raw hourly gusts are strongly serially dependent, as consecutive hours typically belong to the same storm system, whereas seasonal maxima would leave only about $35$ observations per station per season over $1991$--$2025$, too few to estimate pairwise dependence stably. Daily maxima retain on the order of ninety observations per station per season while reducing the most immediate within-day clustering.

An alternative is the peaks-over-threshold (POT) framework, in which exceedances above a high threshold are approximated by a generalised Pareto distribution \citep{balkema1974,pickands1975}. For hourly data, consecutive exceedances usually belong to the same storm and require declustering \citep{ferro2003} or explicit temporal modelling \citep{fawcett2006}. Because daily aggregation already addresses the most immediate serial dependence, our paper treats POT and declustering as robustness alternatives rather than the main strategy.

The marginal step is only the first part of the problem. The spatial question is: conditional on one station observing an extreme daily maximum wind gust, how likely is another station to observe an extreme value on the same day? This shifts the focus to spatial extremal dependence, whose estimands are pairwise tail-dependence measures, extremal coefficients, and dependence--distance curves.

\subsection{Spatial extremes and max-stable processes}\label{sec:lit-spatial}

For block maxima observed over space, max-stable processes provide the standard limiting framework, arising as the limit of pointwise maxima of independent replications of a spatial random field, just as the GEV distribution arises as the limit of scalar maxima. Extremal dependence thereby becomes a spatial object, described as a function of location and distance. \citet{dehaan2006} give a foundational treatment of stationary max-stable models, showing how spatial extremal dependence can be governed by a few parameters related to inter-station distance. Their stationarity assumption imposes a homogeneous dependence structure, which may be restrictive for Dutch gusts, since winter and summer storms need not share the same spatial pattern.

\citet{davison2012} review the main approaches to spatial extremes and conclude that good marginal modelling is not sufficient: models that fit univariate extremes well may still misrepresent joint extremes across locations. This motivates our focus on extremal-dependence summaries rather than marginal behaviour alone. Full-likelihood inference for max-stable processes is infeasible once the number of locations is moderate, and \citet{padoan2010} replace it with a pairwise composite likelihood, establishing pairwise inference as a standard approach.

In our paper, max-stable processes serve mainly as a theoretical reference point and as the benchmark data-generating process for the simulation study (Section~\ref{sec:lit-sim}), not as a model fitted to the data. We adopt the pairwise logic of \citet{padoan2010}, but the empirical objects are nonparametric pairwise summaries computed directly from the seasonal daily maximum wind gusts.

\subsection{Pairwise extremal-dependence measures}\label{sec:lit-pairwise}

The analysis requires dependence measures interpretable at the station-pair level. \citet{cooley2012} survey such tools, including extremal coefficients, madograms, and tail-dependence summaries, which reduce the high-dimensional dependence problem to pairwise quantities that can be compared across seasons and plotted against distance. In our paper all three are estimated from the seasonal daily maximum wind gusts, separately for winter (DJF) and summer (JJA), with margins transformed separately by station and season so that the comparison reflects differences in spatial dependence rather than in the marginal distributions.

For two locations $s_i$ and $s_j$, the pairwise extremal coefficient $\theta(s_i,s_j)$ lies in $[1,2]$: values close to one indicate strong extremal dependence and values close to two near-independence, so stronger winter dependence corresponds to lower winter $\theta(s_i,s_j)$ at comparable distances. An empirical route to it is the $F$-madogram. \citet{cooley2006} study madogram-based summaries for max-stable random fields; for transformed margins,
\[
    \nu_F(s_i,s_j)
    = \tfrac{1}{2}\,
    \mathbb{E}\!\left[
        \bigl| F_{s_i}\{X(s_i)\} - F_{s_j}\{X(s_j)\} \bigr|
        \right],
\]
where $F_{s_i}$ and $F_{s_j}$ are the marginal distribution functions, and under max-stability
\[
    \theta(s_i,s_j)
    = \frac{1 + 2\,\nu_F(s_i,s_j)}{1 - 2\,\nu_F(s_i,s_j)} .
\]
The $F$-madogram thus gives a direct nonparametric estimate of a dependence measure summarised as a dependence--distance curve. The upper-tail dependence coefficient $\chi$ gives a complementary view. With $U(s_i)$ and $U(s_j)$ the marginally standardised observations on a uniform scale,
\[
    \chi(s_i,s_j)
    = \lim_{u \uparrow 1} \Pr\{U(s_j) > u \mid U(s_i) > u\},
\]
and the finite-level quantity is
\[
    \chi_u(s_i,s_j)
    = \Pr\{U(s_j) > u \mid U(s_i) > u\}.
\]
Computed within each season from daily co-exceedances, $\chi_u$ answers the research question directly: if one KNMI station records an extreme daily maximum wind gust, how likely is another to do so on the same day? Larger $\chi_u$ indicates stronger finite-level co-exceedance, just as smaller $\theta$ indicates stronger extremal dependence. \citet{coles1999} introduce $\chi$ and $\bar\chi$ to distinguish asymptotic dependence from asymptotic independence and discuss their finite-level estimation. \citet{wadsworth2012} and \citet{huser2022} caution that classical max-stable models are natural for asymptotic dependence but that finite data may show weakening dependence at increasingly high levels; our paper therefore reports finite-level dependence on a small grid of thresholds rather than a single threshold.

\subsection{Data-generating processes for the simulation study}\label{sec:lit-sim}

To check whether the estimators recover a true dependence structure, recovery must be measured against a known dependence--distance relation. The pairwise extremal coefficients of max-stable processes are available in closed form, so a simulated field carries a known $\theta(s_i,s_j)$ at every distance against which the estimated curve can be compared. The Brown--Resnick process \citep{brown1983}, characterised through a negative-definite variogram, is a widely used family whose extremal dependence is controlled by that variogram and ranges from strong to near-independent as its range varies, and \citet{dombry2016} provide exact simulation algorithms that ensure the known dependence structure is the one actually simulated.

Our paper uses the Brown--Resnick process as the benchmark data-generating process, since its variogram gives direct control over how extremal dependence decays with distance. The null and alternative designs set its variogram-range parameters so that the two regimes either share a dependence structure or differ by a controlled amount. The Brown--Resnick process is used only as a controlled benchmark whose true dependence is known; we do not assume that the Dutch wind-gust data are Brown--Resnick. The aim is solely to test whether the pairwise estimators recover a known winter-versus-summer difference in spatial extremal dependence.

\subsection{Inference and uncertainty for dependence estimators}\label{sec:lit-inference}

Because our paper compares estimated dependence--distance curves, a difference between two curves is only informative once the uncertainty in each is quantified. Two sources of variability matter. First, the $F$-madogram and $\chi_u$ estimators are empirical means and conditional frequencies computed from a finite number of seasonal daily maxima, so their variance grows as the season-split sample shrinks. Second, although daily aggregation removes within-day clustering, a multi-day storm can produce extreme daily maxima on consecutive days, so daily observations cannot be treated as fully independent.

Our paper therefore quantifies uncertainty by resampling temporal blocks. A year or seasonal-block bootstrap resamples whole years, or whole seasonal segments, with replacement and recomputes the full dependence--distance curves on each resample, so that the resulting variability reflects both the finite sample size and the residual serial dependence within storms. The composite-likelihood framework of \citet{padoan2010} provides asymptotic standard errors but relies on correct parametric specification; the nonparametric summaries used here are instead assessed by block resampling and, in the simulation, by Monte Carlo evaluation against the known truth.

\subsection{Seasonal and non-stationary extremal dependence}\label{sec:lit-nonstationarity}

The seasonal question is a restricted form of non-stationary spatial extremal dependence: instead of one dependence structure for all daily maxima, our paper lets the dependence relation differ between two regimes, with season treated as a binary covariate. \citet{huser2016} develop non-stationary dependence structures for spatial extremes, showing that extremal dependence itself, not only the marginal distribution, can vary with covariates. The seasonal split is a deliberately simple version of this idea and also addresses a stationarity concern: estimating one dependence curve from the unstratified daily maxima would mix large-scale winter storms with more localised summer convective events. Stratifying by season does not remove all non-stationarity, but it makes the working assumption more credible and gives each estimated curve a clearer interpretation.

In summary, \citet{fisher1928}, \citet{gnedenko1943}, \citet{balkema1974}, and \citet{pickands1975} justify modelling tail observations and motivate the daily block-maxima choice; \citet{dehaan2006}, \citet{davison2012}, and \citet{padoan2010} provide the max-stable and pairwise-inference framework; \citet{cooley2006}, \citet{cooley2012}, and \citet{coles1999} motivate the pairwise estimands, with \citet{wadsworth2012} and \citet{huser2022} motivating the threshold-grid robustness check; the Brown--Resnick process \citep{brown1983} with exact simulation \citep{dombry2016} supplies the benchmark for validation; and \citet{huser2016} frames the seasonal comparison as non-stationary extremal dependence. The contribution of our paper is to combine these in a deliberately narrow setting: to establish, by simulation, when a seasonal difference in spatial extremal dependence can be recovered from finite daily maximum wind gusts on a fixed, irregular station network, and then to apply the validated estimators to Dutch wind gusts.
