\section{Literature Review}\label{sec:literature}

This review outlines the theoretical and inferential tools needed to estimate and compare season-specific extremal dependence in spatial extremes. It begins with extreme-value theory, which provides asymptotic models for rare events in the tails of distributions, before introducing max-stable processes as natural models for spatial extremes. Particular attention is given to the Brown--Resnick process, its variogram parametrisation, pairwise composite likelihood inference, and the resulting estimators of extremal dependence.

\subsection{Extreme-value theory and spatial extremes}\label{sec:lit-evt}

Extreme-value theory provides limiting models for the tail behaviour of distributions, rather than for their central bulk. For scalar maxima, the Fisher--Tippett--Gnedenko theorem \citep{fisher1928,gnedenko1943} states that any non-degenerate limit of affinely normalised sample maxima must follow a generalised extreme value (GEV) distribution. A related peaks-over-threshold formulation is provided by the Pickands--Balkema--de Haan theorem \citep{pickands1975,balkema1974}, which motivates the use of the generalised Pareto distribution for threshold exceedances. These results justify modelling marginal extremes using GEV or generalised Pareto distributions and motivate transforming each station's marginal distribution to a common extreme-value scale before modelling dependence. This separation allows spatial extremal dependence to be studied independently of marginal differences between stations.

The marginal limit alone is not sufficient to describe how extremes at different sites occur together. For maxima observed over space, the limiting objects are max-stable processes, which arise as pointwise maxima of independent replications of a random field and extend the GEV limit from a scalar to a field \citep{dehaan2006}. Extremal dependence is therefore indexed by pairs of locations and, under stationarity and isotropy, can be summarised as a function of their separation distance. \citet{davison2012} review the area and argue that a model fitting marginal extremes well may still misrepresent joint extremes. This is the reason our paper models the dependence directly rather than inferring it from marginal fits. Ordinary covariance is not the relevant summary, because it is dominated by the bulk (the 'ordinary' windy days) and need not describe the joint tail.

\subsection{Max-stable process models}\label{sec:lit-maxstable}

Several stationary max-stable families are proposed in the literature. \citet{smith1990} introduced a max-stable model with a deterministic storm profile, and \citet{schlather2002} proposed a more flexible construction based on a stationary latent process. Both are limited as models of how dependence decays. The model Smith et al. proposed, implies overly smooth, near-deterministic extremal surfaces, and the Schlather model cannot approach independence at long range. The Brown--Resnick process \citep{brown1977,kabluchko2009} avoids both limitations. Its extremal dependence is determined by the variogram of an underlying Gaussian process with stationary increments, ranges from complete dependence at zero distance to independence at long range, and is determined by a small number of parameters. \citet{davison2012} use it as the leading stationary model for spatial extremes, which is why it is the model estimated in our paper.

\subsection{The Brown--Resnick process and its variogram}\label{sec:lit-br}

\citet{kabluchko2009} characterise stationary Brown--Resnick fields through negative-definite functions, equivalently through the variogram of the underlying process. With an isotropic powered-exponential variogram
\[
    \gamma(d)=\Bigl(\frac{d}{\rho}\Bigr)^{\alpha},
    \qquad \rho>0,\ \alpha\in(0,2],
\]
the range \(\rho\) sets how slowly extremal dependence decays with distance. A larger \(\rho\) is a larger spatial footprint of extremes, and \(\alpha\) sets the smoothness of that decay. Isotropy means dependence depends on distance alone (whereas anisotropy means it depends on both distance and the direction). Within this variogram family, and for comparable values of \(\alpha\), a larger \(\rho\) corresponds to a slower decay of extremal dependence with distance. In practice, the fitted extremal-coefficient curve provides the clearest comparison across seasons. For our paper, this is the statistical objective to interpret, a seasonal difference in spatial footprint becomes a difference in \(\rho\), so winter storms with broad footprints and summer storms events with narrow ones correspond to \(\rho_{\mathrm W}>\rho_{\mathrm S}\). \citet{davison2013} delimit the scope of such models, because Brown--Resnick fields are asymptotically dependent at all finite distances, whereas environmental extremes can be asymptotically independent, so the model may overstate dependence at the highest levels. This is why our paper reports nonparametric tail diagnostics alongside the fit.

\subsection{Pairwise composite likelihood}\label{sec:lit-cl}

Full-likelihood inference for max-stable processes is generally infeasible: the joint density of the field at \(N\) sites is a sum over all partitions of the sites, which grows exponentially in \(N\) \citep{padoan2010}. \citet{padoan2010} replace the full likelihood by a pairwise composite likelihood,
\[
    \ell(\vartheta)=\sum_{i<j}\sum_{t}\log f_{ij}\bigl(z_{i,t},z_{j,t};\vartheta\bigr),
\]
where \(f_{ij}\) is the bivariate density implied by the model and \(\vartheta=(\rho,\alpha)\). Maximising \(\ell\) gives a consistent and asymptotically normal estimator. This is the pseudo-likelihood, replacing an intractable joint objective by a tractable sum of low-dimensional ones. \citet{huser2013} study composite likelihood specifically for the Brown--Resnick process, including the bivariate density and the option of censoring contributions below a high threshold so that the fit targets the joint tail. Our paper uses the pairwise estimator proposed in \citet{padoan2010} with the Brown--Resnick bivariate density, estimated separately by season.
Simulation provides a controlled setting in which the finite-sample behaviour of the pairwise composite-likelihood estimator can be assessed. Since the true dependence parameters are known under a simulated Brown--Resnick data-generating process, simulation makes it possible to check whether the estimation procedure can recover season-specific differences in extremal dependence before it is applied to empirical wind data. This does not validate the Brown--Resnick model as a description of the observed process, but it does validate the estimator and comparison procedure under the assumptions for which they are designed. Exact simulation algorithms for max-stable processes, such as those developed by \citet{dombry2016}, therefore provide the tools needed to verify whether the pairwise composite likelihood estimator can recover known parameter differences in a controlled data-generating process.


\subsection{Inference for composite-likelihood estimators}\label{sec:lit-inference}

Because \(\ell\) is composite, ordinary likelihood standard errors are not valid. The Godambe information is \(G = H J^{-1}H\), where \(H\) is the sensitivity matrix and \(J\) is the variability matrix of the composite score. The corresponding asymptotic covariance matrix is \(G^{-1}=H^{-1}JH^{-1}\). This requires estimating \(J\) and assumes independent replicates. For the empirical data, the replicates are daily maxima, which remain dependent within a multi-day storm. This is problematic because daily maxima are not necessarily independent temporal replicates: multi-day storms can induce serial dependence even after the spatial field has been standardised. To mitigate this issue, we use the block bootstrap to obtain uncertainty estimates and for the winter--summer comparison. Resampling whole season-years preserves both the within-storm serial dependence and the cross-station dependence among pairs sharing a station. The Godambe form is used as a theoretical reference and as a validation under the independence assumption.

\subsection{Nonparametric extremal-dependence diagnostics}\label{sec:lit-diagnostics}

To validate, compare and check the fit of the nonparametric pairwise summaries, we estimate three related quantities. First, the extremal coefficient \(\theta(s_i,s_j)\in[1,2]\) measures the effective number of independent sites in a pair; the \(F\)-madogram of \citet{cooley2006} estimates it from transformed margins without a parametric model. The finite-level tail-dependence coefficient \(\chi_u(s_i,s_j)\) measures the conditional probability that one site exceeds a high marginal quantile given that the other does. These are standard descriptive tools for extremal dependence; here they are diagnostics, used to check whether the fitted Brown--Resnick curve is consistent with the pairwise estimates across distance and whether dependence weakens at the highest levels in a way the model cannot represent. \citet{davison2013} and \citet{huser2020} survey the asymptotic-independence alternatives that these diagnostics would point to if the fit failed.

\subsection{Seasonal and non-stationary extremal dependence}\label{sec:lit-seasonal}

Allowing dependence to differ between winter and summer is a restricted form of non-stationary extremal dependence, with season as a binary covariate. Rather than fit a single covariate-dependent max-stable process, our paper estimates one Brown--Resnick model per season and compares their parameters. This is a simple device, but it addresses a concrete concern: a single model fitted to all daily maxima would mix large-scale winter storms with summer storms, blurring exactly the contrast of interest. Anisotropic and space-time Brown--Resnick extensions exist --- \citet{buhl2016} estimate an anisotropic space-time version --- and are noted as directions the isotropic, season-by-season design here does not pursue. In what follows, \citet{padoan2010} and \citet{huser2013} supply the estimator, \citet{kabluchko2009} the model, \citet{dombry2016} the exact simulation used to validate the estimator, and \citet{cooley2006} and \citet{coles1999} the diagnostics.
