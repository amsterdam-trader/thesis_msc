\section{Introduction}\label{sec:introduction}

Damaging wind in the Netherlands arrives in two distinct forms. Winter gusts are produced by extratropical cyclones, whose mature wind fields can cover large parts of the country within a few hours, so that many places are extreme at once. Summer gusts are more often produced by convective storms and local downbursts, which are intense but spatially confined, so that one station can record a severe gust while nearby stations do not \citep{gliksman2023european}. For the magnitude of the gust at a single site these may look similar, but for the spatial extent of the event they differ, and that extent determines the impact of the on a national scale. The object of our paper is the spatial extent of wind-gust extremes, and whether it differs between winter and summer in The Netherlands. To answer the question empirically, we use the data of the Royal Netherlands Meteorological Institute (KNMI), which has 33 stations with at least 30 years of meteorological data.

Spatial extent is a question about dependence in the joint tail, not about marginal magnitudes, and ordinary correlation does not tell the full story. The data of just ordinary days will dominate the correlation coefficient, and this scalar will not describe how the most extreme gusts co-occur. The relevant quantity is so-called extremal dependence, which describes how the probability that two sites are jointly extreme behaves as the level rises and as the distance between them grows. Estimating it requires extreme-value methods rather than second-moment summaries, and estimating it as a function of distance requires a spatial model for the tail.

Max-stable processes provide such a methodological framework. They are the limiting fields for spatial maxima, just as the generalised extreme value law is the limit for scalar maxima \citep{dehaan2006,davison2012}. Within this class the Brown--Resnick process \citep{brown1977,kabluchko2009} is the model that our paper will use, which will be described in detail in Section \ref{sec:methodology}. Its extremal dependence is set by the variogram of an underlying Gaussian process, taken as \(\gamma_c(d)=(d/\rho_c)^{\alpha_c}\), in which the range \(\rho_c\) controls how slowly dependence decays with distance. For example, a larger \(\rho_c\) is a larger spatial footprint of extremes, and \(\alpha_c\) controls the smoothness of that decay. The process is used as a parametric approximation to the season-specific dependence structure, not as a claim that daily wind gusts are exactly max-stable. Formulating the question through \(\rho\) turns a comparison of spatial footprints into a comparison of one parameter between seasons, which will become clear in Section \ref{sec:intro-rq}.

The model cannot be fitted by full likelihood once more than a few stations are involved, because the joint density of a max-stable field is intractable in moderate dimension \citep{padoan2010}, since the number of parameters grows with the number of stations in the network. The estimator is therefore the pairwise composite likelihood of \citet{padoan2010}. The joint density is replaced by a sum of bivariate densities over station pairs, which is identified on the KNMI network. Daily maxima are treated as repeated spatial fields, their margins are standardised to a common unit-Fr\'echet scale by station and season, and the Brown--Resnick parameters are estimated separately for winter and summer.

\subsection{Research question}\label{sec:intro-rq}

\begin{quote}\itshape
  Does the spatial range of extremal dependence in Dutch daily maximum wind gusts differ between winter and summer?
\end{quote}

Writing \(\vartheta_c=(\rho_c,\alpha_c)\) for the season-specific Brown--Resnick parameters, the comparison is of the dependence ranges. The expectation that winter storms are more widespread than summer storms, is the one-sided hypothesis
\[
  H_0:\ \rho_{\mathrm W}=\rho_{\mathrm S}
  \qquad\text{against}\qquad
  H_1:\ \rho_{\mathrm W}>\rho_{\mathrm S}.
\]
In plain words, the comparison is thus of estimated parameters and their uncertainty, not only of empirical curves; the implied extremal-coefficient curves \(\theta_c(d)\) are reported to place the parameter difference on the dependence-versus-distance scale.

\subsection{Contribution}\label{sec:intro-contribution}

The contribution is to estimate and validate a season-specific parametric spatial extremes model for Dutch wind gusts. Brown--Resnick dependence parameters are estimated separately for winter and summer by pairwise composite likelihood, the range difference \(\rho_{\mathrm W}-\rho_{\mathrm S}\) is tested by a seasonal-year block bootstrap, and a Monte Carlo study under the observed station geometry establishes whether the estimator and the test recover a known seasonal difference before the same procedure is applied to the KNMI data. The nonparametric tail-dependence coefficient, \(F\)-madogram and empirical extremal coefficient, will be reported because they provide complementary information about the spatial dependence structure. The \(F\) madogram is a nonparametric estimator of the variogram of the underlying Gaussian process, and the extremal coefficient is a nonparametric summary of the tail dependence between two sites.
