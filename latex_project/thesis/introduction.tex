\section{Introduction}\label{sec:introduction}

\subsection{Motivation}\label{sec:intro-motivation}

The Netherlands occupies a low-lying river delta on the southern
edge of the North Sea, with sizeable parts of its territory near or
below mean sea level. The country's relationship with extreme
weather has historically been framed as a relationship with water:
the flood of February 1953, known in Dutch as the
\emph{Watersnoodramp}, killed more than \num{1800} people in Zeeland
and South Holland after dikes failed during a spring tide combined
with a severe North Sea storm,

These engineering norms are the most visible legacy of an
extreme-value tradition that, in the Dutch context, has long been
oriented towards flood risk.

Wind has played a comparatively under-emphasised role in this
tradition, despite the fact that wind extremes contribute directly
to two distinct hazards. First, the surface wind field forces the
storm surge that produced the 1953 disaster: a sustained westerly
wind over the North Sea pushes water against the Dutch coast, and
the joint occurrence of a deep low-pressure system, a long fetch
of strong wind and an astronomical high tide is what brings extreme
sea levels to the coastal defences. Second, the
short-duration peak gusts associated with the same extratropical
cyclones are themselves damaging: roofs, infrastructure, forestry
and transport are all sensitive to peak gust speed rather than to
the hourly mean, and a substantial share of insured natural-hazard
losses in the Netherlands is attributable to wind storm events
rather than to flooding alone.

\subsection{Spatial extremal dependence and the seasonal
question}\label{sec:intro-spatial-dependence}

Wind extremes are spatial events. A single mature extratropical
cyclone can affect almost the entire Dutch land area within a few
hours, producing strong gusts at many stations simultaneously; a
localised summer convective gust front, by contrast, may strike
one station and miss its neighbour twenty kilometres away. The two
events can have indistinguishable peak gust speeds at the point
where they happen to hit a station, yet have very different
implications for aggregate exposure across the country. The
relevant statistical object is therefore not the marginal upper
tail at a single station but the \emph{joint} upper tail across
stations: how strongly does an extreme gust at one location predict
an extreme gust at another, and how does this dependence decay with
distance?

Spatial extreme value theory provides a precise vocabulary for
these questions. The pairwise extremal coefficient \(\theta_{ij}\)
and the upper tail dependence coefficient \(\chi_{ij}\) summarise
the joint behaviour of a station pair at the tail; under
max-stability they are in one-to-one correspondence
\citep{TODO-davison2012,TODO-cooley2012}. Most empirical
applications of these tools to wind data treat the field as
stationary across the calendar year, pooling all observations
together. This obscures a basic physical asymmetry: Dutch winter
gust extremes are dominated by large-scale extratropical cyclones
that are inherently spatial, whereas Dutch summer gust extremes
include a larger share of convective events with much smaller
footprints. There is, accordingly, a reasonable prior that the
\emph{spatial structure} of the upper tail of gust observations
differs between winter and summer.

This thesis asks whether that prior is borne out empirically. The
formal counterpart of letting the dependence structure of a
spatial extreme field vary with a covariate is developed in
\citet{TODO-huser-genton2016}; this thesis treats the seasonal
label as a discrete covariate and estimates the dependence
structure separately on a winter and a summer subsample.

\subsection{Research question}\label{sec:intro-rq}

The main research question of this thesis is:

\begin{quote}\itshape
    Is there seasonal variation in the spatial extremal dependence
    of extreme wind gusts at KNMI station locations in the
    Netherlands?
\end{quote}

The primary empirical comparison is winter (December--February,
DJF) versus summer (June--August, JJA). The question of whether
spatial extremal dependence has changed over the historical record
is treated as a secondary, exploratory sample-split analysis, on
the understanding that the changing KNMI station network makes a
clean long-run statement difficult.

\subsection{Data}\label{sec:intro-data}

The empirical application uses hourly observations from the
network of automatic weather stations operated by the Royal
Netherlands Meteorological Institute (KNMI). The primary variable
is the hourly maximum wind gust, denoted \(\mathrm{FX}\), expressed
in metres per second. Stations are indexed by their location
\(s_i \in \mathbb{R}^2\), with latitude, longitude and a sensor-
height attribute recorded in the station metadata. The hourly
parquet store covers 1951--2026 (76 years, 911 monthly partitions).

% FX is effectively unobserved before 1971 (92.5% missing in 1951,
% 12.8% in 1971). The first year with at least 10 stations
% reporting FX in at least 80% of hours is 1971; with at least 20
% stations, 1981; with at least 30 stations, 1990. A balanced
% panel of 25 stations is achievable from 1991 onwards at the 0.80
% threshold; 38 stations from 2001 onwards.
The contemporary KNMI network contains approximately
\(N \approx 60\) Dutch land and coastal stations active in any
single recent year, and 70 unique station identifiers appear at
least once across the full archive. The station
network is unbalanced: stations have been added and retired over
the sample period, so the set of stations active at any given hour
varies with time. The empirical strategy accounts for this by using
a balanced station subset for the main analysis and treating the
larger unbalanced panel as a robustness check
(Section~\ref{sec:emp-stations}).

The hourly parquet files also contain the hourly mean wind speed
\(\mathrm{FH}\) and the station metadata variables; \(\mathrm{FH}\)
is read alongside \(\mathrm{FX}\) but is not the thesis variable
and does not enter any of the dependence estimators.

\subsection{Methodological direction}\label{sec:intro-method}

The thesis stays within spatial extreme value theory and emphasises
tools that are empirically tractable for an MSc-scale project. We
construct extreme observations from the hourly \(\mathrm{FX}\)
series either as seasonal block maxima or as threshold exceedances,
estimate pairwise extremal dependence between station pairs, and
summarise the dependence as a function of the geographical distance
between stations. The seasonal comparison then reduces to a
comparison of two dependence--distance curves on the same set of
station pairs. Max-stable processes appear as the theoretical
motivation for the pairwise estimators
\citep{TODO-davison2012,TODO-padoan2010,TODO-dehaan2006}, but a
full parametric max-stable fit is treated as an optional extension
rather than as the main contribution. The estimates are accompanied
by robustness checks over station selection, block size or
threshold choice, marginal standardisation, and a calendar-time
sample split.

\subsection{Contribution}\label{sec:intro-contribution}

The contribution of this thesis is empirical rather than
methodological. We provide a focused application of pairwise
spatial extremal dependence estimation to hourly Dutch wind gust
data, and a direct seasonal comparison of the resulting
dependence--distance relationship. The closest existing precedent
is \citet{TODO-bonazzi2012}, who use bivariate extreme-value
copulas on European peak gust footprints from 135 selected
windstorms; the present thesis differs by working at the seasonal
rather than the per-storm level, by using the KNMI network alone,
and by summarising dependence through the extremal coefficient and
the tail dependence coefficient rather than through Pickands'
dependence function. The seasonal contrast itself is the object of
interest: it is informative for any risk-aggregation exercise that
currently treats wind gust extremes as having a single,
time-invariant spatial footprint. The thesis does not draw climate-
change conclusions, and the secondary sample-splitting analysis is
presented as exploratory only.
