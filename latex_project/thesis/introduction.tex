\section{Introduction}\label{sec:introduction}

Damaging wind in the Netherlands arrives in two distinct forms. Winter gusts are produced by extratropical cyclones, whose mature wind fields can cover large parts of the country within a few hours, so that many places are extreme at once. Summer gusts are more often produced by convective storms and local downbursts, which are intense but spatially confined, so that one station can record a severe gust while nearby stations do not \citep{mohr2017,gliksman2023european}. For the magnitude of the gust at a single site these may look alike; for the spatial extent of the event they differ, and that extent determines how much of a network is stressed at the same time. The object of this thesis is the spatial extent of wind-gust extremes, and whether it differs between the two seasons.

Spatial extent is a question about dependence in the joint tail, not about marginal magnitudes, and ordinary correlation does not answer it: correlation is dominated by ordinary days and need not describe how the most extreme gusts co-occur. The relevant quantity is extremal dependence, which describes how the probability that two sites are jointly extreme behaves as the level rises and as the distance between them grows. Estimating it requires extreme-value methods rather than second-moment summaries, and estimating it as a function of distance requires a spatial model for the tail.

Max-stable processes provide such a model: they are the limiting fields for spatial maxima, just as the generalised extreme value law is the limit for scalar maxima \citep{dehaan2006,davison2012}. Within this class the Brown--Resnick process \citep{brown1977,kabluchko2009} is the model used here. Its extremal dependence is set by the variogram of an underlying Gaussian process, taken as \(\gamma_c(d)=(d/\rho_c)^{\alpha_c}\), in which the range \(\rho_c\) controls how slowly dependence decays with distance --- a larger \(\rho_c\) is a larger spatial footprint of extremes --- and \(\alpha_c\) controls the smoothness of that decay. The process is used as a parametric approximation to the season-specific dependence structure, not as a claim that daily wind gusts are exactly max-stable. Formulating the question through \(\rho\) turns a comparison of spatial footprints into a comparison of one interpretable parameter between seasons.

The model cannot be fitted by full likelihood once more than a few stations are involved, because the joint density of a max-stable field is intractable in moderate dimension \citep{padoan2010}. The estimator is therefore the pairwise composite likelihood of \citet{padoan2010}: the intractable joint density is replaced by a sum of bivariate densities over station pairs, which is estimable on the KNMI network and is the same composite-likelihood device used elsewhere in econometrics to replace an infeasible likelihood by a tractable objective. Daily maxima are treated as repeated spatial fields, their margins are standardised to a common unit-Fr\'echet scale by station and season, and the Brown--Resnick parameters are estimated separately for winter and summer.

\subsection{Research question}\label{sec:intro-rq}

\begin{quote}\itshape
  Does the spatial range of extremal dependence in Dutch daily maximum wind gusts differ between winter and summer?
\end{quote}

Writing \(\vartheta_c=(\rho_c,\alpha_c)\) for the season-specific Brown--Resnick parameters, the comparison is of the dependence ranges. The expectation that winter storms have broader footprints than summer convection is the one-sided hypothesis
\[
  H_0:\ \rho_{\mathrm W}=\rho_{\mathrm S}
  \qquad\text{against}\qquad
  H_1:\ \rho_{\mathrm W}>\rho_{\mathrm S}.
\]
The comparison is thus of estimated parameters and their uncertainty, not only of empirical curves; the implied extremal-coefficient curves \(\theta_c(d)\) are reported to place the parameter difference on the dependence-versus-distance scale.

\subsection{Contribution and outline}\label{sec:intro-contribution}

The contribution is to estimate and validate a season-specific parametric spatial extremes model for Dutch wind gusts. Brown--Resnick dependence parameters are estimated separately for winter and summer by pairwise composite likelihood, the range difference \(\rho_{\mathrm W}-\rho_{\mathrm S}\) is tested by a seasonal-year block bootstrap, and a Monte Carlo study under the observed station geometry establishes whether the estimator and the test recover a known seasonal difference before the same procedure is applied to the data. The nonparametric tail-dependence coefficient, \(F\)-madogram and empirical extremal coefficient, which are standard nonparametric summaries of extremal dependence, are retained as diagnostics for the fitted model.
