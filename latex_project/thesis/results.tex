\section{Results}\label{sec:results}

This chapter applies the estimator and inference of Section~\ref{sec:methodology} to the KNMI wind gusts of Section~\ref{sec:data}. Section~\ref{sec:res-sim} reports the Monte Carlo study that checks the procedure under a known data-generating process; these simulation outcomes are still to be determined. Section~\ref{sec:res-fit} gives the fitted seasonal Brown--Resnick parameters for the empirical KNMI application, Section~\ref{sec:res-diag} compares the fit with nonparametric diagnostics, and Section~\ref{sec:res-test} reports the seasonal comparison. The empirical numbers in this version are preliminary point estimates from the non-bootstrap run with the fast starting grid; the final version should replace them by the full-grid estimates and add the season-year block-bootstrap intervals.

\subsection{Fitted seasonal dependence}\label{sec:res-fit}

The station--season GEV margins of Section~\ref{sec:meth-marginals} were fitted separately for the 33 stations and the two seasons, giving 66 station--season marginal models. After transformation to the unit-Fr\'echet scale, the Brown--Resnick pairwise composite likelihood of Section~\ref{sec:meth-cl} was maximised over all \(N(N-1)/2=528\) station pairs in each season. The seasonal panel used for the dependence fit contains 203,506 transformed station-day observations after retaining complete DJF and JJA season-years.

\begin{figure}[t]

    \caption{Fitted seasonal extremal-coefficient curves and pairwise extremal-coefficient diagnostics. Lower \(\theta_c(d)\) means stronger extremal dependence at distance \(d\). The preliminary fit places the winter curve below the summer curve over the observed distance range.}
    \label{fig:res-curves}
\end{figure}

The fitted winter range is \(\widehat\rho_{\mathrm W}=418.76\) km and the fitted summer range is \(\widehat\rho_{\mathrm S}=76.98\) km. The resulting range difference is therefore
\[
    \widehat\Delta_\rho
    = \widehat\rho_{\mathrm W}-\widehat\rho_{\mathrm S}
    = 341.79\text{ km}.
\]
The fitted smoothness parameters are almost identical, with \(\widehat\alpha_{\mathrm W}=0.594\) and \(\widehat\alpha_{\mathrm S}=0.597\). Thus the main seasonal difference is not a change in the shape of the distance decay, but a large change in the range scale. In substantive terms, the preliminary estimates indicate that winter wind-gust extremes are spatially much more coherent than summer wind-gust extremes. This is consistent with winter storms being driven by large-scale synoptic systems, whereas summer gust extremes are more often localised.

The fitted model also implies a clear separation in extremal dependence at representative distances. For example, the Brown--Resnick extremal coefficient is about 1.21 in winter and 1.34 in summer at 50 km, about 1.26 in winter and 1.41 in summer at 100 km, and about 1.36 in winter and 1.56 in summer at the maximum network distance of 326 km. Since lower extremal coefficients correspond to stronger extremal dependence, this again supports a wider winter footprint.

\subsection{Nonparametric diagnostics}\label{sec:res-diag}

Figure~\ref{fig:res-diag-chi} and Figure~\ref{fig:res-diag-theta} compare the fitted Brown--Resnick curves with the nonparametric pairwise diagnostics of Section~\ref{sec:meth-diagnostics}. These diagnostics should be used as the main check that the parametric range comparison is meaningful. Agreement between the binned pairwise cloud and the fitted curve supports the Brown--Resnick summary; systematic divergence would indicate that the fitted range may overstate or understate dependence at some distances.

The preliminary model fit should therefore be read together with these diagnostic plots. If the winter diagnostic points lie below the summer points across most distances, this visually supports the fitted conclusion that winter extremes have the larger spatial footprint. If the finite-level tail-dependence estimates decline more sharply than the Brown--Resnick curve at large distances, this should be reported as a qualification because the Brown--Resnick model is asymptotically dependent at every finite distance.

\subsection{Seasonal comparison and hypothesis test}\label{sec:res-test}

The preliminary non-bootstrap range difference is
\[
    \widehat\Delta_\rho = 341.79\text{ km}.
\]
This is large relative to the maximum inter-station distance of 326.21 km in the retained network. The point estimate therefore strongly supports the descriptive conclusion that the winter dependence range exceeds the summer dependence range. However, the formal one-sided test of \(H_0:\Delta_\rho=0\) against \(H_1:\Delta_\rho>0\) requires the season-year block-bootstrap distribution. Because the bootstrap has not yet been run, the bootstrap percentile, confidence interval and \(p\)-value are to be determined.

At this stage, the empirical result should be stated as a preliminary point-estimate finding: the fitted Brown--Resnick range is substantially larger in winter than in summer, while the smoothness parameters are nearly the same. The final thesis version should replace this paragraph by the full-grid and bootstrap result, including the bootstrap interval for \(\Delta_\rho\) and the one-sided test decision.
