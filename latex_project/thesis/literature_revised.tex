\section{Literature Review}\label{sec:literature}

This review outlines the theoretical, inferential and meteorological literature needed to estimate and compare season-specific extremal dependence in spatial wind-gust extremes. It first motivates the winter--summer comparison through the difference between large-scale European windstorms and more localized convective wind events. It then discusses extreme-value theory, max-stable processes, the Brown--Resnick model, pairwise composite likelihood inference, uncertainty quantification under temporal dependence, and nonparametric diagnostics for checking whether the fitted model captures the relevant joint tail behaviour.

\subsection{Wind-gust extremes and seasonal mechanisms}\label{sec:lit-wind}

Extreme wind gusts are an important European hazard because damaging storms can affect many locations simultaneously and therefore generate spatially aggregated losses. For European extratropical windstorms, \citet{sharkey2020} argue that the spatial extent and severity of an event should be modelled jointly rather than reducing the problem to separate sitewise extremes. Their work is especially relevant here because it treats windstorms as spatial events and shows that the footprint of the most severe events is itself a key object of inference. This supports the central interpretation in this thesis: seasonal differences in the fitted extremal-dependence range are interpreted as differences in the spatial footprint of extreme gusts.

The seasonal split is also physically motivated. Winter wind extremes in north-western Europe are typically associated with synoptic-scale extratropical cyclones, whereas summer extremes are more often connected to thunderstorms, downbursts or other convective mechanisms. Convective windstorms are often intense but spatially localized; \citet{pacey2021} provide a European climatology of severe convective windstorms and document that such events are especially frequent in summer. Existing work on European wind and storm damage, such as \citet{gliksman2023european}, therefore motivates a statistical question about spatial dependence rather than only about marginal gust magnitudes: do winter and summer extremes differ in how widely they occur across the KNMI station network?

\subsection{Extreme-value theory and spatial extremes}\label{sec:lit-evt}

Extreme-value theory provides limiting models for the tail behaviour of distributions, rather than for their central bulk. For scalar maxima, the Fisher--Tippett--Gnedenko theorem \citep{fisher1928,gnedenko1943} states that any non-degenerate limit of affinely normalised sample maxima must follow a generalised extreme value (GEV) distribution. A related peaks-over-threshold formulation is provided by the Pickands--Balkema--de Haan theorem \citep{pickands1975,balkema1974}, which motivates the use of the generalised Pareto distribution for threshold exceedances. Standard applied treatments, such as \citet{coles2001}, use these results to justify modelling marginal extremes before studying dependence. In this thesis, the GEV margins are used as a parsimonious station-season standardisation for daily gust maxima. The purpose is not to claim that every daily maximum is an exact asymptotic block maximum, but to place the margins on a common extreme-value scale so that the dependence model is not driven by differences in local wind climatology.

The marginal limit alone is not sufficient to describe how extremes at different sites occur together. For maxima observed over space, the limiting objects are max-stable processes, which arise as pointwise maxima of independent replications of a random field and extend the GEV limit from a scalar to a field \citep{dehaan2006}. Extremal dependence is therefore indexed by pairs of locations and, under stationarity and isotropy, can be summarised as a function of separation distance. \citet{davison2012} review the area and argue that a model fitting marginal extremes well may still misrepresent joint extremes. This is why this thesis models extremal dependence directly rather than inferring it from marginal fits. Ordinary covariance is not the relevant summary, because it is dominated by ordinary windy days and need not describe the joint tail.

\subsection{Max-stable process models}\label{sec:lit-maxstable}

Several stationary max-stable families have been proposed in the spatial-extremes literature. \citet{smith1990} introduced a max-stable model with deterministic storm profiles, and \citet{schlather2002} proposed a more flexible construction based on a stationary latent process. Both are useful historically but limited as models of distance decay: the Smith model implies very smooth extremal surfaces, while the Schlather model cannot approach independence at long range. The Brown--Resnick process \citep{brown1977,kabluchko2009} avoids these limitations. Its extremal dependence is determined by the variogram of an underlying Gaussian process with stationary increments, ranges from complete dependence at zero distance to independence at long range, and can be summarised by a small number of parameters. \citet{davison2012} present it as one of the leading stationary models for spatial extremes, which is why it is the parametric model estimated in this thesis.

There is also direct precedent for using Brown--Resnick-type models in wind-gust applications. \citet{oesting2017} use bivariate Brown--Resnick processes for statistical post-processing of extreme wind-gust forecasts, with an application to a network of stations in northern Germany. Their application differs from this thesis because the objective is forecast post-processing rather than seasonal comparison, but it is useful evidence that Brown--Resnick dependence structures are considered suitable for spatial wind-gust extremes in a comparable north-western European context.

\subsection{The Brown--Resnick process and its variogram}\label{sec:lit-br}

\citet{kabluchko2009} characterise stationary Brown--Resnick fields through negative-definite functions, equivalently through the variogram of the underlying process. With an isotropic powered-exponential variogram
\[
    \gamma(d)=\Bigl(\frac{d}{\rho}\Bigr)^{\alpha},
    \qquad \rho>0,\ \alpha\in(0,2],
\]
the range \(\rho\) determines how slowly extremal dependence decays with distance, while \(\alpha\) controls the smoothness of that decay. A larger \(\rho\) corresponds to a larger spatial footprint of extremes, conditional on comparable \(\alpha\), and the fitted extremal-coefficient curve gives the clearest comparison across seasons. In this thesis, a seasonal difference in spatial footprint is therefore represented by a difference in \(\rho\): broad winter windstorm footprints and more localized summer convective footprints correspond to \(\rho_{\mathrm W}>\rho_{\mathrm S}\).

The Brown--Resnick process is used here as a defensible baseline model, not as a claim that all wind-gust extremes are exactly max-stable. A key limitation is that Brown--Resnick fields are asymptotically dependent at all finite distances, whereas many environmental extremes may exhibit weakening dependence as the level becomes more extreme. \citet{davison2013} discuss this issue in the spatial-extremes context, and more recent work develops models that can bridge or avoid a fixed asymptotic dependence class. \citet{huseropitzthibaud2017} use Gaussian scale mixtures to bridge asymptotic dependence and independence and apply the idea to wind-speed data; \citet{huserwadsworth2019} propose a spatial model with an unknown extremal dependence class; and \citet{huserwadsworth2022} review modern spatial-extremes methods designed to overcome the rigidity of classical max-stable models. These papers motivate the diagnostic part of this thesis: if the Brown--Resnick curve overstates finite-level joint exceedance probabilities, this should be visible in the nonparametric tail-dependence summaries.

\subsection{Pairwise composite likelihood}\label{sec:lit-cl}

Full-likelihood inference for max-stable processes is generally infeasible: the joint density of a field at \(N\) sites is a sum over all partitions of the sites, which grows rapidly with \(N\) \citep{padoan2010}. The standard solution is composite likelihood, which replaces an intractable joint likelihood by a tractable sum of lower-dimensional likelihood contributions. General composite-likelihood theory is reviewed by \citet{varin2011}, who discuss consistency, Godambe information, efficiency and robustness for misspecified likelihoods. In the spatial-extremes setting, \citet{padoan2010} propose pairwise composite likelihood for max-stable processes,
\[
    \ell(\vartheta)=\sum_{i<j}\sum_{t}\log f_{ij}\bigl(z_{i,t},z_{j,t};\vartheta\bigr),
\]
where \(f_{ij}\) is the bivariate density implied by the model and \(\vartheta=(\rho,\alpha)\). Maximising this objective gives a pseudo-likelihood estimator based on all station pairs rather than on the unavailable full joint density.

\citet{huser2013} study composite likelihood specifically for the Brown--Resnick process, including the bivariate density and the option of censoring contributions below a high threshold so that the fit targets the joint tail. \citet{huserdavison2014} extend the discussion to space--time extremes and show how pairwise censored likelihood can be used under mixing conditions. This is relevant because the empirical fields in this thesis are daily spatial observations, and multi-day storms can induce temporal dependence. The estimator used here follows the Brown--Resnick pairwise likelihood line of \citet{padoan2010} and \citet{huser2013}, estimated separately by season.

Simulation provides a controlled setting in which the finite-sample behaviour of the pairwise composite-likelihood estimator can be assessed. Since the true dependence parameters are known under a simulated Brown--Resnick data-generating process, simulation makes it possible to check whether the estimation procedure can recover season-specific differences in extremal dependence before it is applied to empirical wind data. This does not validate the Brown--Resnick model as a description of the observed process, but it does validate the estimator and comparison procedure under the assumptions for which they are designed. Exact simulation algorithms for max-stable processes, such as those developed by \citet{dombry2016}, therefore provide the tools needed to verify whether the pairwise composite-likelihood estimator can recover known parameter differences in a controlled data-generating process.

\subsection{Inference for composite-likelihood estimators}\label{sec:lit-inference}

Because \(\ell\) is composite, ordinary likelihood standard errors are not valid. Composite-likelihood inference is based on the Godambe information \citep{varin2011},
\[
    G = H J^{-1}H,
\]
where \(H\) is the sensitivity matrix and \(J\) is the variability matrix of the composite score. The corresponding asymptotic covariance matrix is \(G^{-1}=H^{-1}JH^{-1}\). This sandwich form is the theoretical reference for the pairwise estimator, but estimating \(J\) directly requires assumptions on the dependence structure of the replicated fields.

For the empirical wind data, daily maxima are not necessarily independent temporal replicates because large storms can last several days. A day-level bootstrap would therefore understate uncertainty by breaking within-storm temporal dependence and by resampling individual fields as if they were independent. Block-bootstrap ideas for dependent observations go back at least to \citet{kuensch1989}, and \citet{lahiri2003} gives a comprehensive treatment of resampling methods for dependent data. In this thesis, uncertainty is therefore based on a season-year block bootstrap. Resampling whole season-years preserves serial dependence within storm episodes and the contemporaneous cross-station dependence that is central to the spatial-extremes problem. The Godambe form remains useful as a theoretical benchmark, while the bootstrap supplies the reported intervals and the winter--summer comparison.

\subsection{Nonparametric extremal-dependence diagnostics}\label{sec:lit-diagnostics}

To validate, compare and check the fit of the Brown--Resnick model, this thesis estimates nonparametric pairwise summaries of extremal dependence. The extremal coefficient \(\theta(s_i,s_j)\in[1,2]\) measures the effective number of independent sites in a pair, and the \(F\)-madogram of \citet{cooley2006} estimates it from transformed margins without a parametric spatial model. The finite-level tail-dependence coefficient \(\chi_u(s_i,s_j)\) measures the conditional probability that one site exceeds a high marginal quantile given that the other site does. These summaries are diagnostics, not separate estimands. They are used to check whether the fitted Brown--Resnick curve is consistent with pairwise empirical estimates across distance and whether dependence weakens at high levels in a way that the model cannot represent. The modern literature on flexible extremal-dependence classes \citep{huseropitzthibaud2017,huserwadsworth2019,huserwadsworth2022} clarifies what such a failure would mean: it would not necessarily invalidate the seasonal comparison, but it would limit extrapolation to rarer joint events.

\subsection{Seasonal and non-stationary extremal dependence}\label{sec:lit-seasonal}

Allowing dependence to differ between winter and summer is a restricted form of non-stationary extremal dependence, with season as a binary covariate. Rather than fitting one fully covariate-dependent max-stable process, this thesis estimates one Brown--Resnick model per season and compares their parameters. This is a simple design, but it targets the concrete scientific contrast of interest: a single model fitted to all daily maxima would mix large-scale winter windstorms with more localized summer convective events and would blur the seasonal footprint comparison. More general anisotropic and space--time Brown--Resnick extensions exist; for example, \citet{buhl2016} estimate an anisotropic space--time version. These extensions are natural directions for future work, but the isotropic season-by-season design keeps the estimand interpretable for the KNMI network.

In summary, \citet{sharkey2020}, \citet{pacey2021} and \citet{gliksman2023european} motivate the seasonal wind-gust question; \citet{kabluchko2009} and \citet{oesting2017} support the Brown--Resnick modelling choice; \citet{padoan2010}, \citet{huser2013}, \citet{varin2011} and \citet{huserdavison2014} support the pairwise composite-likelihood estimator; \citet{kuensch1989} and \citet{lahiri2003} support the dependent-data bootstrap; and \citet{cooley2006}, \citet{huseropitzthibaud2017}, \citet{huserwadsworth2019} and \citet{huserwadsworth2022} support the nonparametric diagnostics and the discussion of Brown--Resnick limitations.
