\section{Simulation Study}\label{sec:simulation}

\subsection{Purpose}\label{sec:sim-purpose}

The empirical analysis estimates \(\vartheta_{\mathrm W}=(\rho_{\mathrm W},\alpha_{\mathrm W})\) and \(\vartheta_{\mathrm S}=(\rho_{\mathrm S},\alpha_{\mathrm S})\) from a single, seasonally split sample of daily maxima on an irregular station network, and reads the winter--summer comparison from \(\widehat\Delta_\rho=\widehat\rho_{\mathrm W}-\widehat\rho_{\mathrm S}\). The simulation asks whether the estimator of Section~\ref{sec:meth-cl} recovers the parameters, and whether the interval-based one-sided bootstrap test of \(H_0:\rho_{\mathrm W}=\rho_{\mathrm S}\) against \(H_1:\rho_{\mathrm W}>\rho_{\mathrm S}\) has the intended size and power, at a sample size and geometry matching the application. The data are generated from Brown--Resnick processes for which \(\vartheta_c\) and the implied curves \(\theta_c(d)\) are known; this is a controlled truth, not a claim that Dutch gusts are Brown--Resnick. Three quantities are evaluated: bias and root mean squared error of \(\widehat\rho_c\) and \(\widehat\alpha_c\), coverage of the bootstrap intervals, and the size and power of the test of \(H_0\).

\subsection{Spatial design}\label{sec:sim-spatial}

Fields are simulated on the empirical station set, so the simulated and observed inter-station distances coincide. Let \(s_1,\dots,s_N\) be the \(N=33\) retained station locations and \(d_{ij}=\lVert s_i-s_j\rVert\) the great-circle distance in kilometres. Each simulated field is one realisation of the spatial process, the analogue of one day; \(n\) fields are drawn per season, with \(n\) matching the per-season count of daily maxima in the data. Sample size is treated as a design factor, with \(n\) reduced below its empirical value to trace how the estimator degrades as the number of fields shrinks.

\subsection{Brown--Resnick data-generating process}\label{sec:sim-dgp}

The data-generating process is the Brown--Resnick max-stable process \citep{brown1977,kabluchko2009} with unit-Fr\'echet margins and variogram
\[
  \gamma_c(d)=\Bigl(\frac{d}{\rho_c}\Bigr)^{\alpha_c},
  \qquad \rho_c>0,\ \alpha_c\in(0,2],
\]
matching the model of Section~\ref{sec:meth-variogram}. Fields are drawn by the exact algorithm of \citet{dombry2016}, so the realised dependence equals the target rather than an approximation to it. The implied extremal coefficient is \(\theta_c(d)=2\Phi\!\bigl(\tfrac12\sqrt{\gamma_c(d)}\bigr)\), with \(\Phi\) the standard normal distribution function, and the implied finite-level coefficient at level \(u\) is \(\chi_u^{(c)}(d)=(1-2u+u^{\theta_c(d)})/(1-u)\); both serve as known targets for the diagnostics of Section~\ref{sec:meth-diagnostics}.

\subsection{Null and alternative designs}\label{sec:sim-designs}

Two regimes, labelled winter and summer, mirror the empirical comparison. Under the null the regimes share the variogram, \(\rho_{\mathrm W}=\rho_{\mathrm S}\), so there is no seasonal difference; under the alternative the winter range exceeds the summer range, \(\rho_{\mathrm W}>\rho_{\mathrm S}\), which implies \(\theta_{\mathrm W}(d)<\theta_{\mathrm S}(d)\) for \(d>0\): winter extremes are more strongly dependent and remain so over longer distances. In both designs the data-generating smoothness is common to the two regimes, \(\alpha_{\mathrm W}=\alpha_{\mathrm S}\), so the alternative isolates a difference in range. Each regime is then estimated with \(\alpha\) free, matching the empirical model of Section~\ref{sec:meth-comparison}; a simplified design that imposes a single common \(\alpha\) in estimation is run as well, since fixing the shared smoothness can sharpen the comparison of \(\rho\). The parameter values are chosen so that the two curves are distinguishable over the distances the network spans, and are reported with the results; they are not empirical estimates.

\subsection{Estimation in each replication}\label{sec:sim-estimation}

Each Monte Carlo replication applies the empirical pipeline unchanged. One winter and one summer sample of \(n\) fields are drawn. Within each regime the margins are transformed as in Section~\ref{sec:meth-marginals}; on exact unit-Fr\'echet fields this step is close to the identity, so applying it keeps the marginal estimation error of the empirical procedure in the simulation rather than removing it. The parameters are estimated by the pairwise composite likelihood of Section~\ref{sec:meth-cl}, giving \(\widehat\vartheta_{\mathrm W}\), \(\widehat\vartheta_{\mathrm S}\) and \(\widehat\Delta_\rho\), and the seasonal-year block bootstrap of Section~\ref{sec:meth-inference} is run within the replication by arranging the \(n\) fields into pseudo-season-years and resampling them. Pointwise targets and the test decision use the same rules as the application. The nonparametric \(F\)-madogram and \(\chi_u\) coefficients \citep{cooley2006} are also computed and compared to their known curves, as a secondary check on the diagnostics. Algorithm~\ref{alg:sim} collects the per-replication procedure.

\begin{algorithm}[t]
  \caption{Monte Carlo evaluation of the seasonal Brown--Resnick estimator}
  \label{alg:sim}
  \begin{algorithmic}[1]
    \Require locations \(\{s_i\}_{i=1}^N\); per-season size \(n\); ranges \(\rho_{\mathrm W},\rho_{\mathrm S}\) and smoothness \(\alpha\); replications \(R\); bootstrap size \(B\); parameter space \(\Theta\)
    \For{\(r=1,\dots,R\)}
    \For{\(c\in\{\mathrm W,\mathrm S\}\)}
    \State draw \(n\) exact unit-Fr\'echet Brown--Resnick fields with variogram \(\gamma_c(d)=(d/\rho_c)^{\alpha}\)
    \State transform margins by station to the unit-Fr\'echet scale (Section~\ref{sec:meth-marginals})
    \State \(\widehat\vartheta_c\gets\arg\max_{\vartheta\in\Theta}\ell_c(\vartheta)\) by pairwise composite likelihood (Section~\ref{sec:meth-cl})
    \EndFor
    \State \(\widehat\Delta_\rho\gets\widehat\rho_{\mathrm W}-\widehat\rho_{\mathrm S}\)
    \For{\(b=1,\dots,B\)}
    \State arrange each regime's \(n\) fields into pseudo-season-years; resample within regime
    \State recompute \(\widehat\vartheta_{\mathrm W}^{*},\widehat\vartheta_{\mathrm S}^{*},\widehat\Delta_\rho^{*}\)
    \EndFor
    \State store \(\widehat\vartheta_{\mathrm W},\widehat\vartheta_{\mathrm S},\widehat\Delta_\rho\), their percentile intervals, and the interval-based one-sided test decision for \(H_0\)
    \EndFor
    \Ensure across \(r\): bias and RMSE of \(\widehat\rho_c,\widehat\alpha_c,\widehat\Delta_\rho\); interval coverage; size (null) and power (alternative)
  \end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Performance measures}\label{sec:sim-performance}

Let \(\mathbb E_{\mathrm{MC}}\) denote the average over the \(R\) replications. For each season-specific parameter \(\beta\in\{\rho_c,\alpha_c\}\) and for \(\Delta_\rho\) the simulation reports
\[
  \operatorname{Bias}(\beta)=\mathbb E_{\mathrm{MC}}[\widehat\beta]-\beta,
  \qquad
  \operatorname{RMSE}(\beta)=\bigl\{\mathbb E_{\mathrm{MC}}[(\widehat\beta-\beta)^2]\bigr\}^{1/2}.
\]
Interval coverage is the proportion of replications whose bootstrap percentile interval for \(\beta\) contains the true value. For the interval-based one-sided bootstrap test of \(H_0:\rho_{\mathrm W}=\rho_{\mathrm S}\) against \(H_1:\rho_{\mathrm W}>\rho_{\mathrm S}\) (Section~\ref{sec:meth-inference}), size is the rejection rate under the null design and should sit near the nominal level, and power is the rejection rate under the alternative. Curve recovery is summarised by the bias and RMSE of the implied extremal coefficient \(\widehat\theta_c(d_0)\) at reference distances \(d_0\), evaluated against \(\theta_c(d_0)\). Because the fields are drawn independently across days, the coverage and size figures are for the favourable case of no residual temporal dependence; this is restated in Section~\ref{sec:sim-interpretation}. Table~\ref{tab:sim-results} collects the parameter results and Table~\ref{tab:sim-test} the size and power.

\subsection{Interpretation}\label{sec:sim-interpretation}

The simulation fixes what the empirical comparison can support. Small bias and near-nominal coverage at the empirical sample size would mean \(\widehat\Delta_\rho\) and its interval can be read directly; the power results map the smallest difference in dependence range that is detectable at this sample size and geometry, which is the relevant quantity for an irregular network with a short tail sample. Two limits are expected in the numbers. The range \(\rho\) is typically estimated less precisely than \(\alpha\), and precision in \(\rho\) deteriorates once the network's maximum distance is small relative to \(\rho\), since the extremal coefficient is then close to its small-distance values across all pairs and carries little information about the range. And because the simulated fields are independent across days, the coverage and size figures are an upper bound on what the seasonal-year bootstrap attains on the data, where consecutive daily maxima within a storm remain dependent; closing that gap would require simulating temporally dependent daily maxima, which is not needed to evaluate recovery of a known spatial difference and is left as an extension.
