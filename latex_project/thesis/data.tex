\section{Data}\label{sec:data}

The empirical analysis uses daily maximum wind gusts recorded at KNMI weather stations in the Netherlands. The sample runs from 1 January 1991 to 1 February 2026 and contains \(N=33\) stations, each with more than \(95\%\) non-missing daily observations over the period. This rule gives a nearly balanced spatial panel while retaining the geographical coverage needed to estimate how extremal dependence varies with distance.

Let \(X_{i,t}\) be the daily maximum wind gust at station \(i=1,\dots,N\) on day \(t\). Station \(i\) has location \(s_i\in\mathbb R^2\), and \(d_{ij}=\lVert s_i-s_j\rVert\) is the great-circle distance between stations \(i\) and \(j\). On each day the network forms one spatial field
\[
      X_t=(X_{1,t},\dots,X_{N,t}),
\]
and the season-specific collection of these fields is the input to the Brown--Resnick estimation of Section~\ref{sec:methodology}. The spatial coverage is summarised by the inter-station distances \(d_{ij}\), whose range determines the distances over which the dependence parameters can be identified.

The analysis is run separately for winter (December--February) and summer (June--August); spring and autumn are not used. December of year \(y\) is assigned to winter \(y+1\), so each winter is one contiguous block, and incomplete boundary seasons are dropped. Coastal and inland stations differ in their gust distributions, and the two seasons differ as well; to keep the comparison about spatial dependence rather than these marginal differences, the margins are standardised separately by station and season before any dependence is estimated. The standardisation is defined in Section~\ref{sec:meth-marginals}.
